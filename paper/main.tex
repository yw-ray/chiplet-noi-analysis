\title{Predict, Place, Refine: Non-Locality-Guided Express Link Placement for LLM Chiplet Networks}

\author{
\IEEEauthorblockN{Youngwoo Jeong}
}

\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
We argue that a single static workload property---the \textit{non-locality fraction} (NL\%, the share of traffic volume between non-adjacent chiplet pairs)---predicts both how much express links help a chiplet network-on-interposer (NoI) and when learned placement is worth invoking over a strong topology-aware heuristic. Building on this predictor, we organize express-link placement around three steps: \textsc{Predict} with NL\%, \textsc{Place} with a workload-aware greedy allocator, and \textsc{Refine} with warm-start reinforcement learning guided by a rate-aware surrogate and validated by post-hoc BookSim selection.

We first prove that center-link amplification under adjacent-only routing grows as $\Theta(K^{3/2})$ and that no routing algorithm eliminates this structural tax, motivating express links as a topological rather than allocation-level fix. Across \textbf{28 BookSim configurations} spanning seven LLM-relevant workloads (NL\% from 10\% to 91\%) and two chiplet counts $K\!\in\!\{16,32\}$ at per-chiplet mesh sizes $N\!\in\!\{4,8\}$, our \textbf{RL-WS} method---multi-warm-start REINFORCE with rate-aware surrogate v2 and top-$k$ BookSim validation---improves latency by \textbf{35.6\%} on average over adjacent-uniform no-express baselines, exceeding both the workload-aware greedy heuristic (+24.8\%) and a Flattened-Butterfly (FBfly) topology-aware allocator (+27.1\%) that is the strongest hand-engineered baseline. RL-WS strictly beats FBfly on \textbf{24/28} cells (2 ties, 2 minor losses $\le$0.9\,cycle); the best single cell reaches \textbf{$-83.2\%$} latency vs FBfly on MoE Skewed, $K$=32, $N$=8, $b$=4$\times$ (67.8 vs 402.8 cycles). A post-hoc BookSim fallback that selects $\min(\text{greedy}, \text{FBfly}, \text{RL})$ at the measured-latency level activates on $4/28$ cells with per-cell rescues of $0.22$--$0.87$ cycle, converting raw-RL regressions on low-NL cells into a measured zero-regression guarantee versus the deterministic baselines. Pooled across the 28 cells, NL\% predicts RL-WS saving with Spearman $\rho{=}0.83$ ($p=6.8\times 10^{-8}$). The pattern is sharply NL\%-stratified: on the 16 high-NL cells (NL$\ge$77\%, including MoE/Hybrid TP+PP/Uniform Random/All-to-all), RL-WS strictly dominates FBfly; on the 12 low-NL cells (Ring/Pipeline/Tree at NL$\le$50\%), the measurable gap to FBfly shrinks to single-cycle scale: RL-WS still strictly beats FBfly on 8/12 with max $-5.3\%$ margin, while the other 4 are ties or minor losses recovered by the fallback. NL\% therefore acts as a \textit{deployment classifier}---a single static statistic that tells architects when traffic-aware learned placement is essential versus when a topology-aware heuristic suffices.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
% ============================================================

AI accelerator chiplet counts are scaling rapidly: AMD's MI300X integrates 8 compute chiplets~\cite{mi300x}, NVIDIA's B200 connects 2 dies~\cite{blackwell}, and industry roadmaps project 16--64 chiplets in next-generation designs. Each additional chiplet demands inter-chiplet bandwidth provisioned through the Network-on-Interposer (NoI)---typically by adding die-to-die links only between adjacent chiplets on the silicon interposer~\cite{kite,florets,ucie}.

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.95\columnwidth]{figures/fig_intro_motivation.pdf}
\caption{The phantom load problem. (a)~Center-link amplification grows super-linearly with chiplet count. (b)~Adding adjacent links yields diminishing returns: at $K$=16, 4$\times$ budget gives only 1.5$\times$ speedup.}
\label{fig:motivation}
\end{figure}

However, adding adjacent links has sharply diminishing returns (Fig.~\ref{fig:motivation}). In a 2D chiplet grid, non-adjacent traffic must traverse intermediate links via multi-hop routing, creating \textit{phantom load}: intermediate links carry routing traffic far exceeding their direct demand. We prove that center links in a $K$-chiplet square grid carry $\Theta(K^{3/2})$ times more traffic than their direct demand. At $K$=32, this amplification reaches 64$\times$ on center links, forcing designers to provision far more adjacent links than would be needed if traffic could reach its destination directly.

Prior NoI work~\cite{kite,florets} optimizes allocation among adjacent pairs---choosing \textit{which} adjacent links receive more capacity. But phantom load is not merely an allocation problem; it is a topological one. Express links---direct interposer connections between non-adjacent chiplets---attack the root cause by bypassing overloaded intermediate links. Topology-aware regular allocators such as Flattened Butterfly (FBfly), in which links are spread regularly across rows and columns, exploit symmetry to reduce phantom load without inspecting the actual traffic matrix. Recent learning-based NoI synthesis~\cite{parl} trains reinforcement learning from scratch on a multi-objective interference score, but without a workload-level predictor for \textit{when} learned placement actually beats a topology-aware regular allocator.

This paper organizes express-link placement around three steps: \textsc{Predict}, \textsc{Place}, and \textsc{Refine}. \textsc{Predict} asks whether a workload has enough non-local traffic for express links to matter at all, using NL\% as a single scalar. \textsc{Place} produces a strong deterministic allocation via workload-aware greedy search and the topology-aware FBfly baseline. \textsc{Refine} improves on both via warm-start reinforcement learning trained against a rate-aware surrogate and validated by post-hoc BookSim selection over $k$ candidates. The three steps are complementary: NL\% predicts \textit{both} the raw express-link benefit \textit{and} when learned RL placement is worth invoking over a topology-aware heuristic.

Our contributions map directly onto these three steps:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{C1. Predict: NL\% as a deployment classifier.} We define NL\% on the pre-routing demand matrix and show that it predicts express-link saving with Spearman $\rho=0.83$ across 28 workload-configuration pairs spanning seven LLM-relevant workloads, and---more importantly---that the \textit{margin} of RL-WS over a topology-aware FBfly allocator is sharply NL\%-stratified. On 16/16 high-NL cells (NL$\ge$77\%) RL-WS strictly beats FBfly; on 12 low-NL cells (NL$\le$50\%) FBfly's row/column regularity is already near-optimal and RL-WS matches within $\pm$1\,cycle. NL\% therefore tells architects \textit{when} to invest in learned placement versus when a topology-aware heuristic suffices. We complement this with a closed-form $\Theta(K^{3/2})$ phantom-load bound and routing-algorithm independence results that isolate the structural role of non-adjacent traffic.
\item \textbf{C2. Place: Two complementary deterministic baselines.} We use both a workload-aware greedy allocator with traffic-proportional budget fallback and a topology-aware Flattened Butterfly (FBfly) allocator that distributes links regularly across rows and columns. The two baselines are complementary by design: greedy is strongest on workloads with skewed traffic (e.g., MoE Skewed: greedy +61.0\% vs FBfly +40.5\%), while FBfly is strongest on workloads with near-uniform high-NL traffic (e.g., Uniform Random: FBfly +35.6\% vs greedy +28.4\%). RL-WS warm-starts from \textit{both} so its search spans the strengths of either heuristic.
\item \textbf{C3. Refine: Multi-warm-start RL with rate-aware surrogate.} We introduce RL-WS, a swap-based REINFORCE policy trained against a rate-aware MLP surrogate (501-dim input including link rate and budget) and warm-started from \textit{both} greedy and FBfly initializations across 16 random seeds, with the top-3 surrogate-predicted candidates per seed validated by BookSim. RL-WS attains a 35.6\% mean latency saving versus adjacent-uniform across 28 cells (versus 24.8\% for greedy alone), strictly beats FBfly on 24/28 cells, and reaches a best-case latency reduction of \textbf{83.2\%} versus FBfly on MoE Skewed $K{=}32, N{=}8, b{=}4\times$ (67.8 vs 402.8 cycles).
\end{itemize}

\section{Background and Related Work}
% ============================================================

\textbf{Adjacent-only NoI design.}
Kite~\cite{kite} and Florets~\cite{florets} optimize adjacent-only chiplet topologies and link allocation. These approaches improve link placement among neighboring chiplets but cannot remove the multi-hop structure that creates phantom load in the first place. We use a \textit{Flattened Butterfly} (FBfly)-style allocator---inspired by Florets's regular-row/column placement and the classical Flattened Butterfly NoC topology---as the strongest topology-aware baseline in our evaluation; it is workload-blind but exploits grid symmetry to spread phantom load evenly.

\textbf{Express channels in monolithic NoC.}
Express Virtual Channels~\cite{express_vc} and concentrated meshes~\cite{cmesh} bypass intermediate hops within a single die. In monolithic NoCs, wire delay is relatively small and the optimization target is hop count. In chiplet NoI, each inter-chiplet link consumes scarce die-edge PHY area and interposer routing resources, so the problem is not just latency but the \textit{cost} of transit traffic.

\textbf{Learning-based topology synthesis.}
The closest prior work is PARL~\cite{parl}, which applies cold-start maskable PPO to NoI topology synthesis for mixed DL workloads on heterogeneous MI300X-class chiplets, optimizing a multi-objective Interference Score that combines latency and bandwidth across multi-tenant workloads. PARL operates in a different problem regime than ours along three axes that are independently consequential. \textbf{(i) Workload predictor.} PARL has no workload-level predictor for \textit{when} learned placement actually beats a topology-aware regular allocator: every workload triggers a full RL run regardless of whether the demand structure has any non-local component to exploit. Our NL\%-based deployment classifier (Section~\ref{sec:nl}) tells the architect, before any RL run, that on NL$\le$50\% workloads (Tree, Ring, Pipeline) FBfly is already within $\pm$1 cycle of RL-WS, so the RL budget can be skipped. \textbf{(ii) Initialization.} PARL trains from cold start, which our internal cold-vs-warm comparison (and the iter-12 ablation that informed our design) shows is unstable on structured workloads such as Tree All-Reduce: cold-RL can regress versus a workload-aware greedy by $>10\%$ on adversarial cells, while the same policy seeded from greedy-or-FBfly closes that worst case. The 17 greedy-warm + 11 FBfly-warm split in our Table~\ref{tab:ablation} shows that \textit{both} warm-start sources are individually necessary in practice. \textbf{(iii) Safety guarantee.} PARL's RL output is the final allocation; ours is selected by post-hoc BookSim measurement against the deterministic baselines (Eq.~\ref{eq:fallback}), which empirically activates on $4/28$ cells and bounds raw-RL regression to zero on every cell. A direct end-to-end reproduction of PARL on our 28-cell benchmark would require porting its Interference-Score reward to BookSim and assembling a multi-tenant workload mix that fits its evaluation contract; we leave that to future work and position the design-space differences in Table~\ref{tab:related}.

\section{Phantom Load Analysis}
% ============================================================

\subsection{Physical Substrate and Chiplet Grid}

\begin{figure*}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.98\textwidth]{figures/fig_interposer_structure.pdf}
\caption{Physical substrate and the source of phantom load. (a)~Vertical cross-section: chiplets sit on a silicon interposer that contains a small number of metal routing layers (typically 3--6 in current CoWoS); adjacent links are short horizontal wires while an express link is a longer wire that may traverse vias across layers. (b)~Top-down view of a $4\times 4$ chiplet grid with only adjacent links: communication between corner chiplets S and D requires six hops, and every intermediate link must carry that transit traffic (phantom load). (c)~Adding a single distance-3 express link (green) absorbs three of the intermediate hops in one physical wire, eliminating phantom load on those links. Our paper asks which non-adjacent pairs should receive such express wires under a fixed interposer routing budget.}
\label{fig:interposer}
\end{figure*}

Fig.~\ref{fig:interposer}(a) shows the physical substrate assumed throughout the paper: a \textbf{2.5D chiplet package} in which multiple dies sit side-by-side on a silicon interposer, as realized by TSMC CoWoS-S/L and Intel EMIB. $K$ compute chiplets sit on a silicon interposer; communication between chiplets goes through the interposer's metal routing layers, which in current CoWoS-class packages expose only 3--6 signal layers---an order of magnitude fewer than a server-class PCB. UCIe 1.x and 2.0 D2D PHY specifications~\cite{ucie} are strictly point-to-point, so every inter-chiplet link is a dedicated wire between exactly two chiplets, and no on-interposer switching fabric exists in ratified standards. Combined with reticle-bounded interposer area (currently $\le 2500$\,mm$^2$), the dominant integration paradigm in commercial chiplet products (AMD MI300~\cite{mi300x}, NVIDIA B200~\cite{blackwell}, Apple M-series UltraFusion, Intel Ponte Vecchio) is a mesh-like layout of compute chiplets with point-to-point UCIe/D2D connectivity.

In this paradigm, traffic between non-adjacent chiplets must traverse multiple physical links in sequence. Fig.~\ref{fig:interposer}(b) illustrates the resulting phantom load on a $4\times 4$ grid: a single S$\to$D flow between diagonally opposite chiplets forces six intermediate links to carry transit traffic for which they have no endpoint demand. An \textit{express link}---a long direct wire on an unused routing channel between a pair of non-adjacent chiplets---absorbs several of these hops at once (Fig.~\ref{fig:interposer}(c)). Physically, the express link consumes interposer wire area proportional to its length, may transition between metal layers through vias, and requires a UCIe PHY at each end (one per chiplet beachfront slot). The total interposer wire budget is therefore a scarce, shared resource, and the central question of this paper is: \textit{given a fixed routing budget and a known workload, which non-adjacent pairs should receive an express link?}

\subsection{System Model}

We consider $K$ chiplets arranged on an $R \times C$ 2D grid on a silicon interposer (e.g., $K$=16 in a 4$\times$4 grid). Each chiplet pair $(i,j)$ has traffic demand $T[i,j]$ (GB/s). In conventional NoI, links connect only \textit{adjacent} pairs---chiplets at Manhattan distance 1. When chiplet $i$ communicates with non-adjacent chiplet $j$, the traffic must traverse a multi-hop path through intermediate chiplets.

The \textit{load} on an adjacent link $(a,b)$ is the total traffic from \textit{all} flows whose routing path crosses it:
\begin{equation}
\text{Load}(a,b) = \sum_{\substack{(i,j): \text{path}(i,j) \\ \text{crosses } (a,b)}} T[i,j].
\end{equation}

We define the \textit{amplification factor}
\begin{equation}
\alpha(a,b) = \frac{\text{Load}(a,b)}{T_{\text{direct}}(a,b)},
\end{equation}
where $T_{\text{direct}}(a,b)$ is the traffic directly exchanged by chiplets $a$ and $b$. Large $\alpha$ indicates that the link is dominated by phantom load rather than its own endpoint demand. Fig.~\ref{fig:phantom4x4} concretizes this on a 4$\times$4 grid: a single long-distance flow C0$\to$C15 traverses six adjacent links, five of which are pure transit; adding one express link at distance $d=3$ bypasses two of those transit links at once.

\subsection{Closed-Form Scaling}

We derive an exact formula for the number of flows crossing each link under XY routing with uniform all-to-all traffic.

\begin{theorem}[Flow count at link boundaries]
Under XY routing with uniform all-to-all traffic on an $R \times C$ grid, the number of directed flows crossing each link is
\begin{align}
F_H(c) &= 2R \cdot (c{+}1)(C{-}c{-}1), \\
F_V(r) &= 2C \cdot (r{+}1)(R{-}r{-}1),
\end{align}
for a horizontal boundary at column $c|c{+}1$ and a vertical boundary at row $r|r{+}1$, respectively.
Since each adjacent pair contributes exactly 2 direct flows, the center-link amplification factor satisfies
\begin{equation}
\alpha_{\max} \;=\; R \cdot \lceil C/2 \rceil \cdot \lfloor C/2 \rfloor.
\end{equation}
\end{theorem}

\begin{corollary}[$\Theta(K^{3/2})$ scaling for square grids]
\label{cor:theta}
For square grids with $R=C=\sqrt{K}$, $\alpha_{\max} = \sqrt{K} \cdot \lfloor K/4 \rfloor = \Theta(K^{3/2})$; center-link amplification therefore grows super-linearly with chiplet count.
\end{corollary}

\textbf{Example.} On a 4$\times$4 grid, the center horizontal link at $c$=1 carries $F_H(1) = 2 \times 4 \times 2 \times 2 = 32$ directed flows, and since each adjacent pair contributes only 2 direct flows, amplification is $\alpha = 32/2 = 16\times$. Table~\ref{tab:scaling} validates Corollary~\ref{cor:theta} across grid sizes up to $K$=64.

\section{Non-Locality Analysis}
\label{sec:nl}
% ============================================================

We define the \textit{non-locality fraction} on demand matrix $T$ and chiplet graph $G$ as
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{NL\%}(T,G) =
\frac{\sum_{i<j:\,\mathrm{hop}_G(i,j)\ge 2} \left(T[i,j] + T[j,i]\right)}
{\sum_{i<j} \left(T[i,j] + T[j,i]\right)}.
\end{equation}
NL\% depends on \textit{both} the demand matrix $T$ and the chiplet-to-grid mapping $G$ (through $\mathrm{hop}_G$), so it is a property of the (workload, layout) pair rather than the workload alone. We compute NL\% under the canonical row-major rank-to-chiplet mapping used throughout the paper; aggressive layout-aware co-location (e.g., placing communicating tensor-parallel ranks on adjacent chiplets) will reduce NL\%, and \textit{this is desirable}---a layout that drops NL\% below the deployment threshold is exactly what the predictor is telling architects to look for. We treat layout-aware NL\% minimization as an orthogonal complementary direction; this paper fixes the layout and asks which links to add.

\textbf{NL\% predicts measured saving.}
Empirically, NL\% correlates strongly with RL-WS express-link saving across the full evaluation set. Pooling all 28 workload-configuration cells (7 workloads $\times$ $\{K\!=\!16,32\}$ $\times$ $\{N\!=\!4,8\}$, primarily at $b{=}4\times$), the Spearman rank correlation between NL\% and RL-WS saving versus adjacent-uniform is $\rho=0.83$ ($p=6.8\times 10^{-8}$). The correlation is stable under method substitution: replacing RL-WS with greedy yields a similar rank ordering, indicating that NL\% predicts the \textit{raw express-link benefit} rather than a property of any specific placement method. More importantly, NL\% also predicts the \textit{margin} of RL-WS over the FBfly topology-aware allocator (Section~\ref{subsec:main}): on high-NL\% cells (NL$\ge$77\%), RL-WS strictly dominates FBfly with a mean latency reduction of $-19.0\%$ (16/16 wins); on low-NL\% cells (NL$\le$50\%), the measurable gap shrinks to single-cycle scale---RL-WS still strictly beats FBfly on 8/12, with the remaining 4 recovered by the post-hoc fallback (Eq.~\ref{eq:fallback}). NL\% therefore acts as a \textit{deployment classifier}: a single static statistic tells architects whether traffic-aware RL placement is essential or whether a topology-aware heuristic suffices.

\section{Express Link Placement}
% ============================================================

\subsection{System Overview}

We augment the adjacent-only NoI with \textit{express links}---direct interposer connections between non-adjacent chiplets. Adjacent links are mandatory and provide baseline connectivity. Express links are optional and may connect chiplets at Manhattan distance $d \in [2, D]$. A single express link at distance $d$ can bypass $d{-}1$ intermediate adjacent links, directly reducing phantom load rather than merely redistributing it.

\subsection{Greedy Baseline}

Given a traffic matrix and link budget, the greedy baseline starts from mandatory adjacent connectivity and repeatedly adds the link---adjacent or express---that most reduces $\rhomax$. Candidate links are limited to a maximum express distance $D$, and routing is re-evaluated on the chiplet-level graph after every tentative addition. When no single addition strictly improves $\rhomax$, the remaining budget is allocated proportionally to traffic demand, with any residue distributed to adjacent links so that the full budget is always consumed.

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{Greedy Express Link Placement}
\label{alg:greedy_express}
\footnotesize
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\REQUIRE Grid $G$, traffic $T$, budget $L$, max distance $D$
\ENSURE Link allocation $A$
\STATE $A[p] \leftarrow 1$ for all adjacent pairs $p$
\STATE $C \leftarrow \{(i,j): \mathrm{hop}(i,j) \le D\}$
\WHILE{budget remaining}
    \FORALL{$c \in C$}
        \STATE Route traffic on $A \cup \{c\}$ and compute $\rhomax$
    \ENDFOR
    \STATE Add $\arg\min_c \rhomax$ if it improves congestion;
    \STATE otherwise distribute the remaining budget proportionally and terminate
\ENDWHILE
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

This baseline is strong, deterministic, and fast, but it remains a local optimum: once no single extra link helps, greedy cannot explore \textit{replacing} one express link with another. We do \textit{not} claim any constant-factor approximation guarantee for Algorithm~\ref{alg:greedy_express} relative to the optimal topology; its role in the paper is as an empirically strong and reproducible starting point, which the multi-warm-start RL refinement in Section~\ref{sec:rl_ws} extends further by also seeding from FBfly and selecting the BookSim-best of the resulting candidates.

\subsection{Multi-Warm-Start RL Refinement}
\label{sec:rl_ws}

We propose \textbf{RL-WS}, a reinforcement-learning refinement initialized from \textit{both} the greedy and FBfly solutions in parallel, trained against a rate-aware surrogate, and finalized by direct BookSim selection over the surrogate-best $k$ candidates.

\textbf{Multi-warm-start initialization.} Rather than warm-starting from a single allocation, RL-WS launches 16 independent training runs per cell: 8 seeded from the greedy allocation and 8 seeded from the FBfly allocation. This dual warm-start lets the policy search diverse neighborhoods and matters because greedy and FBfly are strong on disjoint workload regimes (greedy excels on skewed traffic, FBfly excels on near-uniform high-NL traffic). Each run performs a sequence of remove-add swap actions on chiplet pairs that preserves total budget and per-pair cap.

\textbf{Rate-aware surrogate.} The reward signal comes from a 3-layer MLP surrogate trained on 1408 BookSim samples that span all evaluated $(\text{workload}, K, N, b)$ cells. The input is 501-dimensional: 496 normalized non-adjacent-pair traffic features plus the budget multiplier $b$, the express-link fraction, $K$, $N$, and $\log(\text{rate})$. Adding the link-rate feature lets a single surrogate handle the four BookSim injection rates ($r\!\in\!\{1{,}2{,}3{,}4\}\times r_0$) used during training without per-rate retraining. The surrogate achieves Spearman $\rho{=}0.928$ on a held-out 20\% validation split (val MSE $1924$, vs $\rho{=}0.902$ for an earlier rate-blind v1 surrogate). The split is uniform-random within the full sample pool, so each (workload, $K$, $N$, $b$) cell is represented in both train and validation: the surrogate is a well-matched in-distribution reward model for refinement, not a held-out generalization predictor. We rely on the post-hoc BookSim selection step (Eq.~\ref{eq:fallback}) for the actual safety guarantee, so surrogate generalization error is bounded only as well as it needs to rank candidates within a cell.

\textbf{State, action, reward.} The RL state concatenates the normalized traffic vector and the current link-allocation vector. The action space is a remove-add swap over chiplet pairs. The reward at each step is the surrogate-predicted latency reduction, weighted across the four training rates with weights $\{1{:}1, 2{:}1, 3{:}1, 4{:}2\}$ to emphasize the high-rate regime where express links matter most. We use REINFORCE for 1000 episodes per warm-start run with entropy coefficient 0.01 to encourage exploration. Each run retains the top-3 surrogate-predicted allocations encountered during exploration via a min-heap. After all 16 runs complete, we obtain $16{\times}3{=}48$ surrogate-best candidates per cell.

\textbf{Final BookSim selection with post-hoc fallback.} All 48 RL candidates \textit{and} the two deterministic baselines (greedy, FBfly) are simulated in BookSim at the four training rates, and the candidate with the lowest max-rate latency is selected. This makes the final selection \textit{measured} rather than surrogate-predicted (surrogate error cannot produce a silent regression) and is the post-hoc fallback that gives the safety guarantee
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:fallback}
L_{\text{RL-WS}} = \min\bigl(L_{\text{greedy}}^{\text{measured}},\; L_{\text{FBfly}}^{\text{measured}},\; L_{\text{RL-best}}^{\text{measured}}\bigr).
\end{equation}
Empirically the fallback activates on 4/28 cells (rescuing $0.22$ to $0.87$ cycles when raw-RL would otherwise lose by $\le$0.9\,cycle to a baseline; see Section~\ref{subsec:ablation}); on the remaining 24 cells the BookSim-best is an RL candidate. With fallback active, RL-WS measured latency is by construction never worse than $\min(\text{greedy}, \text{FBfly})$.

\textbf{Why RL helps.} Greedy only considers one-step additions and FBfly is workload-blind. RL-WS reallocates budget across pairs once the global traffic structure is captured, which is most useful when NL\% is high and several long-distance bottlenecks compete for a fixed budget---exactly where Section~\ref{subsec:main} shows the largest measured gains.

\subsection{Setup}

We use BookSim~2.0~\cite{booksim} for cycle-accurate NoI simulation. Each of $K$ chiplets contains an $N \times N$ internal mesh ($N \in \{4,8\}$), where $N$ is also the maximum number of physical links per chiplet pair. We evaluate \textbf{seven workloads} (Table~\ref{tab:workloads_full}) at $K\!\in\!\{16,32\}$ and $N\!\in\!\{4,8\}$, primarily at link-budget $b{=}4\times$ where express placement is most informative; the smallest cell (Tree, $K{=}32, N{=}8$) is reported at $b{=}2\times$ for budget-frontier comparability. This yields \textbf{28 main-result configurations}.

\textbf{Workload non-locality stratification.} Across the seven workloads, NL\% spans an order of magnitude (Table~\ref{tab:workloads_full}). We stratify the analysis into a \textit{high-NL} group (NL$\ge$77\%: Hybrid TP+PP, MoE Skewed, Uniform Random, All-to-all) and a \textit{low-NL} group (NL$\le$50\%: Tree All-Reduce, Ring All-Reduce, Pipeline Parallel). High-NL workloads have most demand falling on non-adjacent pairs and therefore benefit substantially from express links; low-NL workloads have most demand concentrated on adjacent pairs and benefit little.

\textbf{MoE Skewed calibration.} The MoE Skewed workload models top-2 expert dispatch under Zipfian expert popularity. Each chiplet sends activations to 2 of $K{-}1$ candidate expert chiplets, sampled with probability $\Pr(\text{expert at rank } r) \propto 1/r^{s}$ for $s{=}1.5$. This Zipf exponent is consistent with measured expert-popularity skew in production MoE language models---DeepSeek-V3~\cite{deepseekv3} reports concentration ratios in the same regime, and Mixtral-8x7B~\cite{mixtral} shows comparable hot-expert dominance under top-2 routing. Earlier MoE NoI studies that assumed \textit{uniform} top-$k$ dispatch~\cite{gshard} are dominated by load balancing rather than placement, and would conflate the two effects. We average the dispatch matrix across $n_\text{seeds}{=}5$ random samples to suppress single-sample noise. Because Zipf$(1.5)$ places approximately 36\% of total dispatch on the top-2 ranks at $K{=}32$, the resulting traffic matrix is both \textit{highly non-local} (NL\%${=}91$) and \textit{strongly skewed}: a topology-aware regular allocator like FBfly under-provisions the heavy expert pairs by construction, which is exactly where Section~\ref{subsec:percell} reports the largest RL-WS gains.

\textbf{Baseline definitions.} (1) \textit{Adjacent-uniform no-express}: spreads the budget uniformly over adjacent pairs only. (2) \textit{Greedy} (Algorithm~\ref{alg:greedy_express}): workload-aware greedy congestion minimization with traffic-proportional fallback. (3) \textit{FBfly}: a topology-aware Flattened-Butterfly-style allocator that distributes the link budget regularly across rows and columns up to a per-pair cap of $N$, ignoring the workload-specific traffic matrix. FBfly is the strongest deterministic baseline because regular row/column allocation closely matches the symmetric phantom-load distribution induced by uniform-like demand.

\textbf{Methods compared.} (1) Adjacent-uniform no-express; (2) greedy express placement; (3) FBfly; (4) RL-WS (proposed): multi-warm-start REINFORCE policy trained against a rate-aware MLP surrogate (501-dim input including normalized traffic, link rate, and budget) for 1000 episodes, with 16 random seeds (8 greedy-warm-start + 8 FBfly-warm-start), top-3 surrogate-best candidates per seed, and final selection by BookSim measurement.

\textbf{Metrics.} Our main metric is latency saving versus the adjacent-uniform no-express baseline at the same total budget:
\begin{equation}
\text{Saving vs Adj} = \frac{L_{\text{adj}} - L_{\text{method}}}{L_{\text{adj}}}.
\end{equation}
For ablations and unseen-workload comparisons, we also report relative improvement versus greedy:
\begin{equation}
\text{Improvement vs Greedy} = \frac{L_{\text{greedy}} - L_{\text{method}}}{L_{\text{greedy}}}.
\end{equation}
Positive values are better for both metrics.

\textbf{Low-budget regime.} At $b=2\times$, express links consume budget that would otherwise reinforce adjacent capacity. For highly non-local workloads, we observe a \textit{crossover}: below roughly $3\times$, express placement may tie or slightly trail adjacent-uniform because adjacent capacity is starved before express shortcuts become effective. We keep $2\times$ in the evaluation because it is the canonical starting point of hardware budget sweeps and because it exposes the crossover behavior explicitly rather than hiding it behind best-budget selection.

\subsection{Main Result: RL-WS Beats FBfly on High-NL Workloads}
\label{subsec:main}

Table~\ref{tab:main_result} reports the per-workload mean latency saving versus the adjacent-uniform no-express baseline at the same total budget, alongside the head-to-head comparison against FBfly. Three points stand out:

\textbf{(1) RL-WS dominates on high-NL workloads.} On the four high-NL\% groups (NL$\ge$77\%: Hybrid TP+PP, MoE Skewed, Uniform Random, All-to-all), RL-WS strictly beats FBfly on \textit{all} 16 cells. The MoE Skewed workload shows the most dramatic gap: at $K{=}32, N{=}8, b{=}4\times$, FBfly's regular row/column allocation saturates at 402.8 cycles while RL-WS attains 67.8 cycles---an 83.2\% latency reduction.
\textbf{(2) On low-NL workloads, RL-WS still wins narrowly; the gap is small.} On the three low-NL\% groups (NL$\le$50\%: Tree, Ring, Pipeline), demand is concentrated on adjacent pairs and FBfly's row/column regularity is already a strong allocation. RL-WS still strictly beats FBfly on 8/12 of these cells, but the per-cell margin is small (max $-5.3\%$, typically within 1--2\,cycles); the remaining 4 cells split into 2 ties and 2 minor losses by $\le$0.9\,cycle, all recovered by post-hoc fallback. The qualitative claim is therefore not ``FBfly is optimal at low NL\%'' but ``the measurable gap shrinks to single-cycle scale, so a topology-aware heuristic is a defensible default and the additional RL effort is hard to justify per cycle gained.''
\textbf{(3) NL\%-stratified deployment criterion.} The pattern empirically validates NL\% as a \textit{deployment classifier}: when NL\%$\ge$77\%, RL-WS yields large measurable gains over the strongest topology-aware heuristic; when NL\%$\le$50\%, a topology-aware heuristic such as FBfly already captures most of the benefit and the additional cost of running RL is hard to justify.

\subsection{Per-Cell Analysis: When RL-WS Wins by Large Margins}
\label{subsec:percell}

The aggregate numbers in Table~\ref{tab:main_result} are dominated by four MoE Skewed cells; we honestly disclose this distribution rather than letting the headline obscure it.
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{MoE Skewed, $K{=}32$, $N{=}4$, $b{=}4\times$}: 42.2 vs 272.9 cycles ($-84.6\%$).
\item \textbf{MoE Skewed, $K{=}32$, $N{=}8$, $b{=}4\times$}: 67.8 vs 402.8 cycles ($-83.2\%$).
\item \textbf{MoE Skewed, $K{=}16$, $N{=}8$, $b{=}4\times$}: 54.3 vs 198.6 cycles ($-72.6\%$).
\item \textbf{MoE Skewed, $K{=}16$, $N{=}4$, $b{=}4\times$}: 35.2 vs 40.1 cycles ($-12.0\%$).

\textbf{RL-WS still wins on most non-MoE high-NL cells, but by smaller margins.} On the 12 non-MoE high-NL cells (Hybrid TP+PP, Uniform Random, All-to-all), RL-WS strictly beats FBfly on all 12 with a per-cell mean reduction of $-4.3\%$ and a per-cell maximum of $-6.8\%$. The mechanism is the same as on MoE---RL-WS reallocates capacity from low-traffic pairs that FBfly fills regularly to higher-demand pairs---but the heavy-pair concentration is mild enough that the improvement is several percent rather than several factor.

\textbf{Where RL-WS does not strictly beat FBfly.} On 4/28 cells (Tree $K{=}16, N{=}4$ at $b{=}4\times$; Tree $K{=}32, N{=}8$ at $b{=}2\times$; Pipeline $K{=}16, N{=}4$ at $b{=}4\times$; Ring $K{=}16, N{=}4$ at $b{=}4\times$) RL-WS does not strictly beat FBfly: 2 are ties within $\pm$0.1\,cycle and 2 are minor losses by $\le$0.9\,cycle. All four are NL$\le$50\% cells where adjacent demand dominates and FBfly's regular allocation already maps onto the actual phantom-load pattern; in this regime RL-WS produces equivalent allocations within the stochasticity of REINFORCE+surrogate. This is the empirical justification for using NL\% as a deployment classifier: at NL$\le$50\% there is no consistent measurable gap to chase.

\section{Discussion}
% ============================================================

\textbf{A Predict--Place--Refine workflow.} The three contributions compose into a clean design-time workflow. \textsc{Predict}: compute NL\% from the workload's demand matrix. If NL\%$\le$50\%, express links are unlikely to pay off relative to a topology-aware regular allocator (FBfly) and additional learned-placement effort is hard to justify. \textsc{Place}: at higher NL\%, both workload-aware greedy and FBfly produce strong deterministic allocations in seconds; FBfly is the better single-shot default on near-uniform high-NL traffic. \textsc{Refine}: when NL\%$\ge$77\% \textit{and} the traffic is also skewed (most clearly MoE-style sparse top-$k$ dispatch), the FBfly--RL gap is large (up to $-83\%$ on MoE $K{=}32, N{=}8$) and RL-WS is essential rather than incremental. NL\% therefore is not just a predictor of \textit{whether} express links help---it is a deployment classifier for \textit{which} placement strategy to invoke.

\textbf{Design-time trade-off.} Greedy and FBfly run in seconds and form strong deterministic baselines. RL-WS takes minutes per configuration (1000 episodes $\times$ 16 warm-start seeds plus BookSim selection over $\le$48 candidates), but uniquely captures workload-specific skew that no fixed topology can express. The cost is justified precisely on the high-NL skewed-traffic cells where the measured gap to FBfly is large.

\textbf{Why mesh + express, not switch-based topologies?}
At the board level, GPU-GPU fabrics such as NVSwitch demonstrate that switch-based interconnects scale well; however, these live in discrete ASICs between packages and are outside the scope of chiplet NoI. Within a single package, commercial chiplet products all use point-to-point or mesh-like inter-chiplet connectivity because (i) UCIe 1.x/2.0 D2D specs are strictly point-to-point with no switching layer in the ratified standards, (ii) the reticle-bounded silicon interposer ($\le 2500$\,mm$^2$ in current CoWoS) leaves no spare area for a dedicated switch chiplet once compute tiles and HBM stacks are placed, and (iii) silicon interposers (CoWoS, EMIB) expose \textit{fewer} routing metal layers than package substrates or PCBs (typically 3--6 vs 8--30), so the dense centralized wiring a switch fabric requires does not fit on-interposer. Academic proposals for routing/switch chiplets remain prototypes. Our mesh + express formulation thus matches the dominant chiplet integration paradigm; switch-based intra-package fabrics are a complementary research direction rather than a competing baseline.

\textbf{Deadlock freedom.}
The combined topology remains deadlock-free under XY-style dimension-ordered routing on the chiplet-level graph, provided each express link is assigned to the same virtual channel class as the dimension whose hop count it reduces (so an express link at horizontal distance $d$ travels in the $X$-VC subset, and similarly for $Y$). With two VC subsets (one per dimension), the ordered channel-dependency graph on (VC, direction) pairs remains acyclic: a packet only consumes $X$-VCs before $Y$-VCs, and express links, whose hop equivalence is purely along one dimension, do not introduce new cycles into this order. A formal Duato-style proof is outside the scope of this paper, but the above construction requires no new routing primitives beyond those already present in BookSim's anynet topology support.

\textbf{Sensitivity to the express-link latency model.}
Our BookSim runs adopt a linear wire-delay model in which a distance-$d$ express link has latency $2d$ cycles. This is the standard assumption in prior NoI work~\cite{kite,florets} and reflects CoWoS-class wire delay scaling. To verify that our headline savings are not an artifact of this specific assumption, we re-run BookSim on the four best-budget $K{=}32, N{=}8$ cells (one per workload) with the per-hop express latency scaled by a factor $\lambda\in\{1.0, 1.5, 2.0\}$; adjacent-link latencies are left at the baseline 2-cycle value. Table~\ref{tab:lambda_sensitivity} reports the resulting greedy and RL-WS+fb savings. Across all twelve (workload, $\lambda$) cells, savings remain positive and large: Tree All-Reduce drops from $+17.7\%$ to $+14.3\%$ ($\lambda{=}1.0\!\to\!2.0$), Hybrid TP+PP from $+47.5\%$ to $+41.9\%$, Uniform Random from $+46.9\%$ to $+42.3\%$, and MoE Skewed from $+56.5\%$ to $+52.5\%$. Relative erosion is modest (between $11\%$ and $19\%$ of the $\lambda{=}1.0$ saving), and the NL\%-ordered ranking of savings is preserved across all three $\lambda$ values. Notably, the RL-WS uplift over greedy \textit{grows} under higher $\lambda$ on high-NL\% workloads (Uniform: $+3.1\,\text{pp}\!\to\!+5.8\,\text{pp}$; MoE: $+4.4\,\text{pp}\!\to\!+4.9\,\text{pp}$), indicating that when wire-delay dominates, workload-aware express placement becomes more valuable, not less. Express saving is therefore not a product of the optimistic $2d$-cycle model; it reflects a structural property of phantom-load reduction.

\textbf{Limitations.} (i) Our workloads are synthetic but workload-inspired rather than traced from full training runs; we leave end-to-end-trace evaluation as future work. (ii) The headline aggregate gap to FBfly is dominated by MoE Skewed: excluding MoE, the per-cell mean reduction across the remaining 24 cells is $-3.2\%$ rather than $-11.8\%$. We do not hide this---the MoE dependence is exactly what the deployment classifier is meant to identify, and Section~\ref{subsec:percell} discloses it explicitly. (iii) NL\% is layout-dependent (Section~\ref{sec:nl}); the deployment-classifier framing assumes a fixed canonical mapping, and an architect who can reshuffle ranks across chiplets may move a workload across the classifier boundary. We see this as a feature rather than a bug. (iv) The rate-aware surrogate is trained with random 80/20 split over all evaluated cells, so its $\rho{=}0.928$ validation correlation is in-distribution; the safety mechanism that does \textit{not} depend on cross-cell surrogate generalization is the post-hoc BookSim fallback (Eq.~\ref{eq:fallback}), which we rely on for the worst-case guarantee. A leave-one-workload-out surrogate study is future work. (v) RL-WS uses 16 random seeds × top-3 candidate evaluation per cell ($\le$48 BookSim runs); ablation Table~\ref{tab:ablation} isolates the contribution of multi-warm-start and post-hoc fallback, but a multi-run variance study (independent end-to-end RL retraining per cell) is left to future work. (vi) PARL~\cite{parl} targets a complementary problem (multi-tenant interference minimization via full topology synthesis with the Interference Score metric); we position it qualitatively instead of attempting a head-to-head reproduction. (vii) The express-link wire-delay model is linear and analytical; we validate robustness via the $\lambda\!\in\!\{1.0,1.5,2.0\}$ sweep in Table~\ref{tab:lambda_sensitivity} but do not derive per-link latencies from SPICE-level physical simulation. (viii) The paper targets 2.5D chiplet integration on a silicon interposer (CoWoS-S/L or EMIB); extending to 3D die-stacked packages (Foveros-style) with TSV-dominant wire models is future work.

% ============================================================
\section{Conclusion}
% ============================================================

\textbf{Conclusion.} We cast express-link placement for LLM chiplet networks as a three-step problem---\textsc{Predict}, \textsc{Place}, \textsc{Refine}---and show that a single layout-aware workload statistic, the non-locality fraction NL\%, acts as a \textit{deployment classifier} for the third step. NL\% predicts express-link saving with Spearman $\rho=0.83$ across 28 workload-configuration cells covering seven LLM-relevant traffic patterns, and stratifies the value of learned placement: on 16/16 high-NL cells (NL$\ge$77\%) RL-WS strictly beats the strongest topology-aware baseline (FBfly) with a peak reduction of $-83.2\%$ on MoE Skewed $K{=}32, N{=}8, b{=}4\times$; on 12 low-NL cells (NL$\le$50\%) RL-WS still strictly beats FBfly on 8/12 with smaller margins ($\le$2\,cycle), and the 4 cells where it does not are recovered by a post-hoc BookSim fallback that selects $\min(\text{greedy}, \text{FBfly}, \text{RL-best})$ at the measured-latency level. Across all 28 cells RL-WS attains a 35.6\% mean latency saving versus adjacent-uniform, exceeding both greedy (24.8\%) and FBfly (27.1\%) and strictly winning 24/28 head-to-head against FBfly with a measured zero-regression guarantee on the remaining 4. The headline gap to FBfly is dominated by MoE Skewed (excluding MoE the per-cell mean reduction is $-3.2\%$), which we present as a feature rather than a defect: it is exactly the regime where workload-aware adaptation pays off by orders of magnitude, and the NL\%-driven classifier is what tells architects to invoke RL-WS there. Together, these results justify a practical chiplet NoI workflow: compute NL\% first, fall back to FBfly when NL\% is low, and invoke RL-WS whenever NL\%$\ge$77\% indicates that the workload's non-local traffic structure leaves meaningful headroom for traffic-aware adaptation.
