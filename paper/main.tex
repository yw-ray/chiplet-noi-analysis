\title{Breaking the Cost-Performance Ceiling of Chiplet Networks with Express Links}

\author{
\IEEEauthorblockN{Youngwoo Jeong}
}

\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
As chiplet counts in AI accelerators scale toward 16--64, inter-chiplet bandwidth demands grow rapidly. The conventional approach---adding more adjacent links on the interposer---suffers from a hidden cost trap: \textit{phantom load} from multi-hop routing forces designers to provision far more links than topologically necessary. We prove that phantom load amplification grows as $\Theta(K^{3/2})$ for center links in $K$-chiplet square grids, and that no routing algorithm eliminates it.

We propose \textbf{express links}---direct connections between non-adjacent chiplets---with workload-aware greedy placement. Through BookSim cycle-accurate simulation of five LLM communication patterns on $K$=16 and $K$=32 chiplet grids, we show that express link benefit is determined by the workload's \textit{non-locality fraction} (NL\%, the share of traffic between non-adjacent chiplets). At $K$=32 with 8$\times$8 per-chiplet mesh, express links reduce latency by \textbf{57\% for skewed MoE dispatch} (NL=91\%), \textbf{53\% for all-to-all} (NL=90\%), \textbf{51\% for hybrid TP+PP} (NL=77\%), and \textbf{17\% for tree all-reduce} (NL=42\%)---all at the same link budget as adjacent-only baselines. The correlation between NL\% and express saving is $r$=0.93 at $K$=32, $N$=8 ($p$=0.02) and $r$=0.58 ($p$=0.008, Spearman $\rho$=0.67) pooled across all 20 configurations, enabling architects to predict express benefit from static traffic analysis.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
% ============================================================

% --- 1. Trend ---
AI accelerator chiplet counts are scaling rapidly: AMD's MI300X integrates 8 compute chiplets~\cite{mi300x}, NVIDIA's B200 connects 2 dies~\cite{blackwell}, and industry roadmaps project 16--64 chiplets in next-generation designs. Each additional chiplet demands inter-chiplet bandwidth provisioned through the Network-on-Interposer (NoI)---typically by adding die-to-die links between adjacent pairs on the silicon interposer~\cite{kite,florets,ucie}.

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.95\columnwidth]{figures/fig_intro_motivation.pdf}
\caption{The phantom load problem. (a)~Center link amplification grows quadratically with chiplet count. (b)~Adding adjacent links yields diminishing returns: at $K$=16, 4$\times$ budget gives only 1.5$\times$ speedup.}
\label{fig:motivation}
\end{figure}

% --- 2. Problem ---
However, adding adjacent links has sharply diminishing returns (Fig.~\ref{fig:motivation}). In a 2D chiplet grid, non-adjacent traffic must traverse intermediate links via multi-hop routing, creating \textit{phantom load}: intermediate links carry routing traffic far exceeding their direct demand. We prove that center links in a $K$-chiplet square grid carry $\Theta(K^{3/2})$ times more traffic than their direct demand---super-linear growth that makes the problem worsen faster than chiplet count. At $K$=32, this amplification reaches 64$\times$ on center links, forcing designers to provision far more adjacent links than would be needed if traffic could reach its destination directly.

% --- 3. Why existing approaches fail ---
Prior NoI work~\cite{kite,florets} optimizes link allocation among adjacent pairs---choosing \textit{which} adjacent links get more capacity. But phantom load is not an allocation problem; it is a topological one. Traffic-proportional allocation is 2--3$\times$ \textit{worse} than uniform (Table~\ref{tab:mitigation}) because it starves phantom-loaded links that carry no direct traffic.

% --- 4. Our claim ---
We propose \textbf{express links}---direct interposer connections between non-adjacent chiplets---with workload-aware greedy placement. Express links eliminate phantom load at its source: a single express link at distance $d$ relieves $d{-}1$ intermediate links simultaneously. The key result: \textbf{express link benefit is determined by the workload's non-locality fraction}. At $K$=32 with 8$\times$8 per-chiplet mesh, skewed MoE achieves 57\% latency reduction, hybrid TP+PP 51\%, and all-to-all 53\% at the same link budget as adjacent-only baselines. The non-locality fraction (NL\%) predicts express benefit with $r$=0.93 correlation ($K$=32, $N$=8), providing architects a simple metric for topology investment decisions.

Our contributions:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Phantom load analysis}: Closed-form proof of $\Theta(K^{3/2})$ amplification for square grids; routing-algorithm independence across four algorithms; workload sensitivity across five LLM patterns.
\item \textbf{Non-locality--driven express benefit}: BookSim-validated express savings of 17--57\% across five workloads (including skewed MoE and TP=8 hybrid), two chiplet counts ($K$=16, 32), and two mesh sizes ($N$=4, 8), with NL\% as the dominant predictor ($r$=0.93 at $K$=32).
\item \textbf{Workload-aware placement}: Greedy allocation with traffic-proportional fallback achieves 100\% budget utilization across all workload types, from sparse MoE (129 express links at $K$=32) to local tree all-reduce (16 express links).
\end{itemize}

\section{Related Work}
% ============================================================

\textbf{NoI Topology Design.}
Kite~\cite{kite} proposes heterogeneous adjacent-only topologies with accurate interconnect modeling, achieving 7\% latency reduction. Florets~\cite{florets} designs data-flow-aware NoI for CNN inference. Both optimize \textit{allocation} among adjacent pairs but cannot address phantom load because the topology remains adjacent-only. We include a Kite-like baseline (MinMax adjacent) and show it saturates identically to uniform at $K$=16. Modular routing~\cite{modular_routing} proposes deadlock-free routing for chiplet systems but does not modify topology.

\textbf{Express Channels in Monolithic NoC.}
Express Virtual Channels~\cite{express_vc} and concentrated meshes~\cite{cmesh} bypass intermediate hops within a single die. In monolithic NoC, wire delay is negligible and routing resources are abundant, so phantom load does not arise as a cost problem. In chiplet NoI, each inter-chiplet link consumes scarce die-edge PHY area and interposer routing resources, making phantom load a \textit{cost} problem---the focus of this work.

\textbf{Commercial Chiplet Systems.}
AMD MI300X ($K$=8) uses Infinity Fabric with near-full connectivity among XCDs. Our analysis shows $K$=8 is in the manageable regime ($\alpha_{\max}$=8). NVIDIA B200 ($K$=2) trivially avoids phantom load. As products scale to $K \geq 16$, our results predict 2--3$\times$ link cost overhead without topology intervention.

\textbf{Chiplet Cost Modeling.}
Chiplet Actuary~\cite{chiplet_actuary} and CATCH~\cite{catch} model manufacturing cost but not communication topology. CPElide~\cite{cpelide} optimizes multi-chiplet GPU synchronization assuming fixed topology. CNSim~\cite{cnsim} provides cycle-accurate chiplet network simulation.

Our work is the first to cover all five: topology optimization with non-adjacent links, link-level cost analysis, BookSim cycle-accurate simulation, evaluation across five LLM workloads, and validation across multiple chiplet counts and internal mesh sizes.

\section{Phantom Load Analysis}
% ============================================================

\subsection{System Model and Definition}

We consider $K$ chiplets arranged on an $R \times C$ 2D grid on a silicon interposer (e.g., $K$=16 in a 4$\times$4 grid). Each chiplet pair $(i,j)$ has a traffic demand $T[i,j]$ (GB/s). In conventional NoI, links connect only \textit{adjacent} pairs---chiplets at Manhattan distance 1. When chiplet $i$ needs to communicate with a non-adjacent chiplet $j$, the traffic must traverse a multi-hop path through intermediate chiplets.

The \textit{load} on an adjacent link $(a,b)$ is the total traffic from \textit{all} flows whose routing path crosses it---not just the direct traffic between $a$ and $b$:
\begin{equation}
\text{Load}(a,b) = \sum_{\substack{(i,j): \text{path}(i,j) \\ \text{crosses } (a,b)}} T[i,j]
\end{equation}

We define the \textit{amplification factor} $\alpha(a,b) = \text{Load}(a,b) / T_{\text{direct}}(a,b)$, where $T_{\text{direct}}$ is the traffic directly between $a$ and $b$. When $\alpha = 1$, the link carries only its own traffic. When $\alpha \gg 1$, the link is dominated by \textit{phantom load}---transit traffic from other flows passing through.

Fig.~\ref{fig:phantom4x4}(a) illustrates a concrete example: when chiplet~0 communicates with chiplet~15 on a 4$\times$4 grid, XY routing produces a 6-hop path (0$\to$1$\to$2$\to$3$\to$7$\to$11$\to$15). All 5 intermediate links carry this flow as phantom load---they must handle traffic that has nothing to do with their directly connected chiplets. When \textit{many} such long-distance flows overlap on the same intermediate links, the amplification factor on center links grows rapidly.

\subsection{Closed-Form Analysis}

We derive an exact formula for the number of flows crossing each link under XY (dimension-order) routing with uniform all-to-all traffic.

\textbf{Intuition.} Consider a horizontal link at the column boundary between columns $c$ and $c{+}1$. Under XY routing, horizontal movement occurs at the source row. Any flow originating from a chiplet in columns $0 \ldots c$ and destined for a chiplet in columns $c{+}1 \ldots C{-}1$ must cross this boundary. Counting all such source-destination pairs (in both directions) yields:

\begin{theorem}
Under XY routing with uniform all-to-all traffic on an $R \times C$ grid, the number of directed flows crossing each link is:
\begin{align}
F_H(c) &= 2R \cdot (c{+}1)(C{-}c{-}1) \quad \text{(horizontal at col $c|c{+}1$)} \\
F_V(r) &= 2C \cdot (r{+}1)(R{-}r{-}1) \quad \text{(vertical at row $r|r{+}1$)}
\end{align}
$F_H$ is independent of row position because XY routing performs all horizontal movement at the source row, so every row contributes equally.
\end{theorem}

\textbf{Example.} On a 4$\times$4 grid ($R$=4, $C$=4), the center horizontal link at $c$=1:
$F_H(1) = 2 \times 4 \times 2 \times 2 = 32$ flows.
Since each adjacent link has only 2 direct flows (one per direction), the amplification is $\alpha = 32 / 2 = 16\times$---the center link must carry 16 times more traffic than its direct demand.

Since each link has 2 direct flows, the \textbf{center link amplification} is:
\begin{equation}
\alpha_{\max} = R \cdot \lceil C/2 \rceil \cdot \lfloor C/2 \rfloor = \Theta(K^{3/2}) \text{ for square grids}
\end{equation}

Table~\ref{tab:scaling} shows the scaling across grid sizes, validated computationally for all $R,C \leq 8$. The amplification grows from $2\times$ at $K$=4 to $128\times$ at $K$=64.

\textbf{Why this is a cost problem.} To keep the most congested link below a utilization target $\rho^*$, an adjacent-only topology must provision $\lceil \alpha_{\max} / \rho^* \rceil$ links on center pairs. At $K$=64, this means $128\times$ more capacity than the direct traffic demand requires---a massive cost overhead in PHY area and interposer routing resources. This overhead is not an implementation inefficiency; it is a \textit{structural tax} imposed by multi-hop routing.

\subsection{Routing Algorithm Independence}

A natural question is whether smarter routing eliminates phantom load. We compare four routing algorithms (Table~\ref{tab:routing}):

\textbf{XY/YX} (deterministic): Route horizontally first then vertically, or vice versa. Both produce the same imbalance (8.2$\times$ at $K$=16) because they simply mirror the load pattern.

\textbf{ECMP} (equal-cost multi-path): Split each flow equally across all shortest paths. This reduces imbalance significantly (8.2$\times$ $\to$ 2.3$\times$ at $K$=16) by distributing load more evenly. However, the center links \textit{still} carry disproportionate load because they lie on more shortest paths than edge links.

\textbf{Valiant} (randomized): Route each flow through a random intermediate chiplet. This reduces imbalance further but \textit{doubles} total network load because each flow traverses two paths. The cure is worse than the disease for cost-sensitive NoI design.

\textbf{Conclusion}: No routing algorithm can eliminate phantom load. ECMP helps but 6.1$\times$ imbalance persists at $K$=32. The fundamental issue is that multi-hop routing forces intermediate links to carry transit traffic regardless of how that traffic is distributed.

\subsection{Workload Sensitivity}

The closed-form analysis assumes uniform all-to-all traffic. Real workloads have structured communication patterns that concentrate or disperse phantom load depending on their \textit{non-locality fraction} (NL\%)---the share of total traffic between non-adjacent chiplet pairs. We evaluate five LLM communication patterns at $K$=32 (Table~\ref{tab:workloads}), spanning a wide NL\% range:

\textbf{Tree all-reduce} (NL=42\%) uses recursive halving-doubling (butterfly) with XOR-based partner selection, creating moderate non-local traffic at power-of-two distances. \textbf{Hybrid TP+PP} (NL=49\%) combines tensor-parallel all-to-all within 4-chiplet groups and pipeline-parallel forwarding between groups; intra-group non-adjacent pairs create substantial non-local traffic.

\textbf{MoE expert dispatch} (NL=88\%) sends each token to top-2 remote experts, creating sparse all-to-all traffic across the entire grid. As MoE architectures become dominant (GPT-4, Mixtral, DeepSeek-V3), this pattern increasingly stresses chiplet NoI. \textbf{Uniform random} (NL=89\%) is a synthetic worst-case baseline. \textbf{All-to-all} (NL=90\%) represents sequence-parallel or context-parallel redistribution where all chiplets exchange data equally.

\section{Express Link Architecture}
% ============================================================

\subsection{System Overview}

We propose augmenting conventional adjacent-only NoI with \textit{express links}---direct interposer connections between non-adjacent chiplets. The system consists of:

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Chiplet grid}: $K$ chiplets on an $R \times C$ grid, each with an $N \times N$ internal mesh providing $N$ border routers per edge.
\item \textbf{Adjacent links}: mandatory connections between neighboring chiplets (latency 2 cycles, $\sim$10\,mm wire).
\item \textbf{Express links}: optional connections between chiplets at Manhattan distance $d \in [2, D]$ (latency $2d$ cycles, $\sim$10$d$\,mm wire). A single express link at distance $d$ relieves phantom load on $d{-}1$ intermediate adjacent links.
\item \textbf{Routing}: Dijkstra shortest-path on the combined topology (adjacent + express), re-computed after each express link addition.
\end{itemize}

Express links reuse the same UCIe/D2D PHY circuits as adjacent links; the longer wire may require stronger drivers, adding $\sim$10--15\% power per link (Table~\ref{tab:physical}). Fig.~\ref{fig:phantom4x4}(b) illustrates the mechanism: an express link from C0 to C3 reduces the C0$\to$C15 path from 6 to 4 hops, freeing 2 intermediate links from phantom load.

\subsection{Workload-Aware Greedy Placement}

Given a traffic matrix and link budget, we place express links via greedy congestion minimization (Algorithm~\ref{alg:greedy}): starting from mandatory adjacent connectivity (one link per pair), iteratively add the link---adjacent or express---that reduces $\rhomax$ most. When no single addition strictly improves $\rhomax$ (greedy plateau), the remaining budget is allocated proportionally to traffic demand across all candidate pairs, with any residual distributed to adjacent links. This ensures \textit{100\% budget utilization} regardless of traffic sparsity. The number of express links adapts to the workload: 129--174 for dense non-local patterns (MoE, all-to-all at $K$=32) and 16 for hierarchical patterns (tree all-reduce).

Complexity is $O(L \cdot |C| \cdot K^2 \log K)$ per iteration, where $|C| = O(K^2)$.

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{Greedy Express Link Placement}
\label{alg:greedy}
\footnotesize
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\REQUIRE Grid $G$, traffic $T$, budget $L$, max distance $D$
\ENSURE Link allocation $A$
\STATE $A[p] \leftarrow 1$ for all adjacent pairs $p$
\STATE $C \leftarrow \{(i,j) : \text{hop}(i,j) \leq D\}$
\WHILE{budget remaining}
    \FORALL{$c \in C$}
        \STATE Route traffic on $A \cup \{c\}$ (Dijkstra); compute $\rhomax$
    \ENDFOR
    \STATE Add $\arg\min_c \rhomax$; \textbf{break} if no improvement
\ENDWHILE
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Baseline Strategies}

We compare against four adjacent-only strategies:
\textbf{(1) Uniform}: equal links to all adjacent pairs;
\textbf{(2) Traffic-proportional}: links $\propto$ direct traffic $T[a,b]$;
\textbf{(3) Load-aware}: links $\propto$ actual load including routing traffic;
\textbf{(4) MinMax adjacent} (Kite-like): minimize $\rhomax$ via binary search, adjacent-only.

Table~\ref{tab:mitigation} compares all strategies. Traffic-proportional is \textit{worse} than uniform (1.5$\times$ at $K$=16) because it assigns zero capacity to phantom-loaded links. Load-aware matches MinMax. Express greedy achieves the lowest $\rhomax$ by eliminating multi-hop routing.

\section{Evaluation}
% ============================================================

\subsection{Setup}

We use BookSim~2.0~\cite{booksim} for cycle-accurate NoI simulation. Each of $K$ chiplets contains an $N \times N$ per-chiplet mesh ($N \in \{4, 8\}$), where $N$ determines the maximum inter-chiplet links per adjacent pair. We evaluate on $K$=16 (4$\times$4 grid) and $K$=32 (4$\times$8 grid) chiplet configurations. Traffic matrices are generated from five LLM communication patterns (Table~\ref{tab:workloads}), with chiplet-level traffic normalized to preserve relative weight magnitudes in BookSim. We sweep the link budget from 1$\times$ to $N\times$ per adjacent pair and measure latency at four injection rates (1--4$\times$ base load, normalized so total injection is constant across configurations).

\textbf{Strategies}: (1) \textit{Adjacent uniform}---equal links to all adjacent pairs; (2) \textit{Express greedy}---workload-aware placement (Algorithm~\ref{alg:greedy}) with traffic-proportional fallback for remaining budget.

\textbf{Metric}: \textit{Express saving} = $(L_{\text{adj}} - L_{\text{expr}}) / L_{\text{adj}}$, where $L$ is latency at maximum injection rate with the same total link budget.

\subsection{Express Benefit Is Workload-Dependent}

Table~\ref{tab:main_result} shows the central result: express saving varies dramatically across workloads and correlates strongly with non-locality fraction. At $K$=32, $N$=8 (2048 nodes), skewed MoE achieves \textbf{57\%} latency reduction, hybrid TP+PP 51\%, all-to-all 53\%, and tree all-reduce 17\%. The pattern is consistent across all four configurations.

Comparing $K$=16 vs.\ $K$=32, saving also increases for high-NL\% workloads---at larger $K$, phantom load is stronger, making express shortcuts more valuable.

\subsection{Adjacent-Only Strategies Cannot Bypass Phantom Load}

A key question is whether smarter \textit{allocation} among adjacent links---without changing the topology---can resolve phantom load. Table~\ref{tab:mitigation} answers definitively: at $K$=16 with 3$\times$ budget, adjacent uniform achieves $\rhomax$=14.3, while traffic-proportional (link count $\propto$ direct traffic) achieves $\rhomax$=23.1---\textit{1.6$\times$ worse}. Traffic-proportional starves phantom-loaded links that carry no direct traffic but high routing load. Express greedy, by contrast, reaches $\rhomax$=6.6 (2.2$\times$ better than uniform). This confirms that the bottleneck is topological, not allocational: no amount of adjacent-link redistribution can compensate for the multi-hop paths that cause phantom load.

\subsection{Ablation: Placement Strategy}

Express links are only effective if placed correctly. Table~\ref{tab:ablation} compares four placement strategies at $K$=16, $N$=4, budget $L$=72 (3$\times$), averaged over 3 random traffic seeds. \textbf{Random} express placement is \textit{worse} than no express at all ($\rhomax$ 18.0 vs.\ 14.3)---carelessly placed express links waste budget without targeting bottlenecks. \textbf{Fully connected} (one link to every pair, distribute remainder equally) achieves $\rhomax$=9.5, better than random but still 1.4$\times$ worse than greedy (6.6), because it spreads budget thinly across all $\binom{K}{2}$ pairs. This confirms that \textit{where} express links are placed matters more than simply having them---traffic-aware placement is essential.

\subsection{Non-Locality Predicts Express Benefit}

Fig.~\ref{fig:nonlocality} plots the non-locality fraction (NL\%) against the best express saving for all 20 workload-configuration combinations. At $K$=32, $N$=8, the Pearson correlation is $r$=0.93 ($p$=0.02, 95\% CI [0.26, 1.00]). Pooled across all 20 points (5 workloads $\times$ 4 configurations), Spearman rank correlation $\rho$=0.67 ($p$=0.001) confirms the monotonic relationship is robust. The pooled correlation is weaker than the $K$=32, $N$=8 slice because at smaller configurations, the link budget is limited by $N$ (at $N$=4, maximum budget is 4$\times$adjacent baseline), suppressing express benefit even for high-NL\% workloads.

This correlation has a practical implication: chiplet architects can predict express link benefit from a static traffic analysis---computing NL\% from the workload's communication graph---without running cycle-accurate simulation. Workloads with NL\%$>$75\% (MoE, hybrid TP+PP, all-to-all) warrant express link investment with 51--57\% saving; workloads with NL\%$<$50\% (tree) see moderate benefit (17\%); and highly local workloads benefit primarily from adjacent capacity.

\textbf{End-to-end impact.} Assuming NoI communication accounts for 10--15\% of LLM training iteration time (typical for MoE dispatch in chiplet-scale accelerators), a 57\% NoI latency reduction translates to \textbf{6--9\% end-to-end training speedup} by Amdahl's law. For the 51\% reduction under hybrid TP+PP, the speedup is 5--8\%.

\subsection{Physical Overhead}

Express links require longer interposer wires than adjacent links. Table~\ref{tab:physical} quantifies the overhead per link based on estimates from CoWoS-class technology parameters (0.8\,$\mu$m wire pitch, 256 signal wires per 32\,GB/s link, 0.15\,pJ/bit/mm wire energy). A distance-3 express link uses a 30\,mm wire consuming 6.1\,mm$^2$ and 1.15\,W---3$\times$ the wire cost of an adjacent link. For 10 express links at average distance 2.5, total overhead is $\sim$56\,mm$^2$ (0.56\% of a 100$\times$100\,mm$^2$ interposer) and $\sim$15\,W (2.1\% of 700\,W TDP). Critically, this cost is more than offset by the \textit{reduction} in total link count: our express topology uses 72 total links (including $\sim$19 express) instead of 168 adjacent-only links, saving $\sim$96 PHY modules ($\sim$48\,mm$^2$ PHY area)---a net area saving despite the longer wires.

% ============================================================
\section{Conclusion}
% ============================================================

\textbf{Conclusion.} We identified phantom load as a fundamental cost problem in chiplet NoI: multi-hop routing forces far more links than topologically necessary, with center-link amplification growing as $\Theta(K^{3/2})$---super-linearly with chiplet count. Express links break this cost-performance ceiling by eliminating phantom load at its source. Through BookSim simulation of five LLM workloads on $K$=16 and $K$=32 grids with $N$=4 and $N$=8 per-chiplet meshes, we showed that express link benefit is governed by the workload's non-locality fraction (NL\%): 57\% latency reduction for skewed MoE (NL=91\%), 51\% for hybrid TP+PP (NL=77\%), 53\% for all-to-all (NL=90\%), and 17\% for tree all-reduce (NL=42\%), with $r$=0.93 at $K$=32, $N$=8 and Spearman $\rho$=0.67 ($p$=0.001) across all 20 configurations. This provides architects a simple, simulation-free predictor for topology investment: compute NL\% from the workload's communication graph, and invest in express links when NL\% exceeds 40\%.
