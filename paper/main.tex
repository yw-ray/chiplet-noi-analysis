\title{Breaking the Cost-Performance Ceiling of LLM Chiplet Networks via Non-Locality-Guided Warm-Started Reinforcement Learning}

\author{
\IEEEauthorblockN{Youngwoo Jeong}
}

\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
As chiplet counts in AI accelerators scale toward 16--64, inter-chiplet bandwidth demands grow rapidly. The conventional approach---adding more adjacent links on the interposer---suffers from a hidden cost trap: \textit{phantom load} from multi-hop routing forces designers to provision far more links than topologically necessary. We prove that center-link amplification in square chiplet grids grows as $\Theta(K^{3/2})$, and that no routing algorithm eliminates this structural tax.

We then identify the workload's \textit{non-locality fraction} (NL\%, the share of traffic volume between non-adjacent chiplet pairs) as the key predictor of express-link benefit, and propose \textbf{RL-WS}: warm-start reinforcement learning that refines greedy express placement with a post-hoc BookSim fallback. Across 40 configurations spanning four workloads, two chiplet counts, two per-chiplet mesh sizes, and multiple link budgets, greedy express placement improves latency by \textbf{25.6\%} on average over adjacent-uniform no-express baselines, while RL-WS raises this to \textbf{28.1\%}. The best single configuration reaches \textbf{56.4\%} latency reduction (MoE, $K$=32, $N$=8, 4$\times$ budget). RL-WS beats greedy in 32/40 configurations and, with fallback, is never worse. On unseen ring-allreduce, pipeline-parallel, and all-to-all workloads, RL-WS wins 6/6 evaluated cases while a zero-shot GNN baseline fails on all-to-all traffic. These results show that NL\% predicts both raw express-link benefit and the remaining headroom beyond greedy placement.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
% ============================================================

AI accelerator chiplet counts are scaling rapidly: AMD's MI300X integrates 8 compute chiplets~\cite{mi300x}, NVIDIA's B200 connects 2 dies~\cite{blackwell}, and industry roadmaps project 16--64 chiplets in next-generation designs. Each additional chiplet demands inter-chiplet bandwidth provisioned through the Network-on-Interposer (NoI)---typically by adding die-to-die links only between adjacent chiplets on the silicon interposer~\cite{kite,florets,ucie}.

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.95\columnwidth]{figures/fig_intro_motivation.pdf}
\caption{The phantom load problem. (a)~Center-link amplification grows super-linearly with chiplet count. (b)~Adding adjacent links yields diminishing returns: at $K$=16, 4$\times$ budget gives only 1.5$\times$ speedup.}
\label{fig:motivation}
\end{figure}

However, adding adjacent links has sharply diminishing returns (Fig.~\ref{fig:motivation}). In a 2D chiplet grid, non-adjacent traffic must traverse intermediate links via multi-hop routing, creating \textit{phantom load}: intermediate links carry routing traffic far exceeding their direct demand. We prove that center links in a $K$-chiplet square grid carry $\Theta(K^{3/2})$ times more traffic than their direct demand. At $K$=32, this amplification reaches 64$\times$ on center links, forcing designers to provision far more adjacent links than would be needed if traffic could reach its destination directly.

Prior NoI work~\cite{kite,florets} optimizes allocation among adjacent pairs---choosing \textit{which} adjacent links receive more capacity. But phantom load is not merely an allocation problem; it is a topological one. Express links---direct interposer connections between non-adjacent chiplets---attack the root cause by bypassing overloaded intermediate links. Our previous greedy express placement results already showed large gains on highly non-local workloads, but also exposed a second problem: greedy remains a local heuristic and leaves headroom on complex traffic patterns.

This paper makes three claims. First, the workload's non-locality fraction (NL\%) is the fundamental predictor of express-link benefit. Second, warm-start reinforcement learning can refine greedy placement without sacrificing its strong baseline behavior. Third, a post-hoc fallback to measured BookSim latency provides a practical worst-case guarantee: the learned method is never worse than greedy.

Our contributions are:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Phantom-load analysis}: We prove $\Theta(K^{3/2})$ center-link amplification for square chiplet grids, quantify routing-algorithm independence, and separate structural phantom load from routing or allocation artifacts.
\item \textbf{Non-locality-guided placement}: We show that NL\% predicts express-link benefit across four representative LLM communication patterns and use it as the organizing principle for placement quality and RL headroom.
\item \textbf{Warm-start RL with safety}: We introduce RL-WS, a swap-based RL refinement initialized from greedy placement and protected by post-hoc fallback. Across 40 configurations, RL-WS improves average saving from 25.6\% to 28.1\%, reaches 56.4\% in the best case, and never regresses once fallback is applied.
\end{itemize}

\section{Background and Related Work}
% ============================================================

\textbf{Adjacent-only NoI design.}
Kite~\cite{kite} and Florets~\cite{florets} optimize adjacent-only chiplet topologies and link allocation. These approaches improve link placement among neighboring chiplets, but they cannot remove the multi-hop structure that creates phantom load in the first place.

\textbf{Express channels in monolithic NoC.}
Express Virtual Channels~\cite{express_vc} and concentrated meshes~\cite{cmesh} bypass intermediate hops within a single die. In monolithic NoCs, wire delay is relatively small and the optimization target is hop count. In chiplet NoI, each inter-chiplet link consumes scarce die-edge PHY area and interposer routing resources, so the main problem is not just latency but the \textit{cost} of transit traffic.

\textbf{Learning-based topology synthesis.}
The closest prior work is PARL~\cite{parl}, which applies cold-start PPO to NoI topology synthesis for mixed DL workloads. PARL optimizes a multi-objective interference score but does not use workload-level non-locality as a predictor, does not warm-start from a strong heuristic, and offers no worst-case guarantee relative to a deterministic baseline. Our method targets express-link placement specifically, starts from greedy placement, and uses post-hoc BookSim fallback to guarantee measured latency no worse than greedy.

\section{Phantom Load Analysis}
% ============================================================

\subsection{System Model}

We consider $K$ chiplets arranged on an $R \times C$ 2D grid on a silicon interposer (e.g., $K$=16 in a 4$\times$4 grid). Each chiplet pair $(i,j)$ has traffic demand $T[i,j]$ (GB/s). In conventional NoI, links connect only \textit{adjacent} pairs---chiplets at Manhattan distance 1. When chiplet $i$ communicates with non-adjacent chiplet $j$, the traffic must traverse a multi-hop path through intermediate chiplets.

The \textit{load} on an adjacent link $(a,b)$ is the total traffic from \textit{all} flows whose routing path crosses it:
\begin{equation}
\text{Load}(a,b) = \sum_{\substack{(i,j): \text{path}(i,j) \\ \text{crosses } (a,b)}} T[i,j].
\end{equation}

We define the \textit{amplification factor}
\begin{equation}
\alpha(a,b) = \frac{\text{Load}(a,b)}{T_{\text{direct}}(a,b)},
\end{equation}
where $T_{\text{direct}}(a,b)$ is the traffic directly exchanged by chiplets $a$ and $b$. Large $\alpha$ indicates that the link is dominated by phantom load rather than its own endpoint demand.

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.95\columnwidth]{figures/fig_phantom_4x4.pdf}
\caption{Phantom load example on a 4$\times$4 chiplet grid. (a)~Adjacent-only: C0$\to$C15 requires 6 hops via XY routing; every intermediate link carries transit traffic. (b)~An express link C0$\to$C3 reduces the path to 4 hops, freeing 2 intermediate links.}
\label{fig:phantom4x4}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Closed-Form Scaling}

We derive an exact formula for the number of flows crossing each link under XY routing with uniform all-to-all traffic.

\begin{theorem}
Under XY routing with uniform all-to-all traffic on an $R \times C$ grid, the number of directed flows crossing each link is
\begin{align}
F_H(c) &= 2R \cdot (c{+}1)(C{-}c{-}1), \\
F_V(r) &= 2C \cdot (r{+}1)(R{-}r{-}1),
\end{align}
for a horizontal boundary at column $c|c{+}1$ and a vertical boundary at row $r|r{+}1$, respectively.
\end{theorem}

\textbf{Example.} On a 4$\times$4 grid, the center horizontal link at $c$=1 carries
\begin{equation}
F_H(1) = 2 \times 4 \times 2 \times 2 = 32
\end{equation}
directed flows. Since each adjacent pair contributes only 2 direct flows, the amplification is $\alpha = 32 / 2 = 16\times$.

Since each adjacent link has 2 direct flows, the center-link amplification is
\begin{equation}
\alpha_{\max} = R \cdot \lceil C/2 \rceil \cdot \lfloor C/2 \rfloor = \Theta(K^{3/2})
\end{equation}
for square grids. Table~\ref{tab:scaling} validates the scaling across grid sizes up to $K$=64.

\textbf{Why this is a cost problem.} To keep the most congested link below utilization target $\rho^*$, an adjacent-only topology must provision $\lceil \alpha_{\max} / \rho^* \rceil$ links on center pairs. At $K$=64, this implies over two orders of magnitude more capacity than direct demand alone would require.

\section{Non-Locality Analysis}
% ============================================================

We define the \textit{non-locality fraction} on demand matrix $T$ and chiplet graph $G$ as
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{NL\%}(T,G) =
\frac{\sum_{i<j:\,\mathrm{hop}_G(i,j)\ge 2} \left(T[i,j] + T[j,i]\right)}
{\sum_{i<j} \left(T[i,j] + T[j,i]\right)}.
\end{equation}
NL\% is computed on the pre-routing demand matrix. It is therefore a static workload property relative to a chosen chiplet layout and lets us predict whether non-adjacent shortcuts are even worth considering before simulation.

We evaluate four representative chiplet communication patterns at $K$=32. These form the training workloads for the learned placement methods; ring-allreduce, pipeline-parallel, and all-to-all are revisited later as unseen generalization workloads.

\label{tab:workloads}
\scriptsize
\begin{tabular}{lrrrrl}
\toprule
Workload & NL\% & Max $\alpha$ & Imbal. & Phantom\% & Pattern \\
\midrule
Tree AR & 42 & 2.2 & 2.2 & 69\% & hierarchical \\
Hybrid TP+PP & 77 & 5.2 & 2.4 & 85\% & mixed TP=8 \\
MoE Skewed & \textbf{91} & \textbf{79.0} & 1.7 & \textbf{100\%} & skewed a2a \\

Tree all-reduce uses recursive halving-doubling and therefore creates only moderate non-local traffic. Hybrid TP+PP increases NL\% by combining tensor-parallel groups with pipeline stages. MoE dispatch and uniform random traffic are both highly non-local; the difference is that MoE is sparse and skewed, while uniform random is dense. This distinction later matters for how much additional headroom remains beyond greedy placement.

\section{Express Link Placement}
% ============================================================

\subsection{System Overview}

We augment the adjacent-only NoI with \textit{express links}---direct interposer connections between non-adjacent chiplets. Adjacent links are mandatory and provide baseline connectivity. Express links are optional and may connect chiplets at Manhattan distance $d \in [2, D]$. A single express link at distance $d$ can bypass $d{-}1$ intermediate adjacent links, directly reducing phantom load rather than merely redistributing it.

\subsection{Greedy Baseline}

Given a traffic matrix and link budget, the greedy baseline starts from mandatory adjacent connectivity and repeatedly adds the link---adjacent or express---that most reduces $\rhomax$. Candidate links are limited to a maximum express distance $D$, and routing is re-evaluated on the chiplet-level graph after every tentative addition. When no single addition strictly improves $\rhomax$, the remaining budget is allocated proportionally to traffic demand, with any residue distributed to adjacent links so that the full budget is always consumed.

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{Greedy Express Link Placement}
\label{alg:greedy_express}
\footnotesize
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\REQUIRE Grid $G$, traffic $T$, budget $L$, max distance $D$
\ENSURE Link allocation $A$
\STATE $A[p] \leftarrow 1$ for all adjacent pairs $p$
\STATE $C \leftarrow \{(i,j): \mathrm{hop}(i,j) \le D\}$
\WHILE{budget remaining}
    \FORALL{$c \in C$}
        \STATE Route traffic on $A \cup \{c\}$ and compute $\rhomax$
    \ENDFOR
    \STATE Add $\arg\min_c \rhomax$ if it improves congestion;
    \STATE otherwise distribute the remaining budget proportionally and terminate
\ENDWHILE
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

This baseline is strong, deterministic, and fast, but it remains a local optimum: once no single extra link helps, greedy cannot explore \textit{replacing} one express link with another.

\subsection{Warm-Started RL Refinement}

We therefore propose \textbf{RL-WS}, a reinforcement-learning refinement initialized from the greedy solution rather than from an empty topology.

\textbf{Warm start.} RL-WS begins from the greedy allocation and performs a sequence of swap actions. Each action removes one existing link from the current allocation and adds one link to a different pair under the same total budget and per-pair cap. This keeps the search in the neighborhood of a strong feasible solution instead of forcing RL to rediscover basic structure from scratch.

\textbf{State, action, and reward.} The RL state concatenates normalized traffic features and the current link-allocation vector. The action space is a remove-add swap over chiplet pairs. A 3-layer MLP surrogate, trained on 288 BookSim samples, predicts latency and provides the reward signal. We use REINFORCE for a few hundred episodes per configuration and keep the best predicted allocation found during exploration.

\textbf{Why RL helps.} Greedy only considers one-step additions. RL-WS can reallocate budget from an already-placed express link to a better target pair once the global traffic pattern becomes clearer. This is most useful when NL\% is high and several long-distance bottlenecks compete for the same budget.

\subsection{Post-Hoc Fallback}

Surrogate-guided RL can still make mistakes. To prevent regressions, we run both the greedy and RL allocations in actual BookSim and keep the lower-latency one. This \textit{post-hoc fallback} turns RL-WS into a safe enhancement over greedy:
\begin{equation}
L_{\text{RL-WS}} \le L_{\text{greedy}}
\end{equation}
for every evaluated configuration by construction.

We also retain a zero-shot GNN allocator as a fast comparison point in the generalization study, but RL-WS is the proposed method because it adapts per configuration and supports the fallback guarantee.

\section{Evaluation}
% ============================================================

\subsection{Setup}

We use BookSim~2.0~\cite{booksim} for cycle-accurate NoI simulation. Each of $K$ chiplets contains an $N \times N$ internal mesh ($N \in \{4,8\}$), where $N$ is also the maximum links per chiplet pair. We evaluate four training workloads (Table~\ref{tab:workloads}) across ten hardware-budget configurations each:
\begin{itemize}
\item $K$=16, $N$=4 with budgets 2$\times$, 3$\times$, 4$\times$
\item $K$=16, $N$=8 with budgets 2$\times$, 4$\times$, 7$\times$
\item $K$=32, $N$=4 with budgets 2$\times$, 4$\times$
\item $K$=32, $N$=8 with budgets 2$\times$, 4$\times$
\end{itemize}
This yields 40 main-result configurations.

\textbf{Baselines.} We compare adjacent-uniform no-express, greedy express placement, RL-WS, and zero-shot GNN where relevant.

\textbf{Metrics.} Our main metric is latency saving versus adjacent-uniform no-express:
\begin{equation}
\text{Saving vs Adj} = \frac{L_{\text{adj}} - L_{\text{method}}}{L_{\text{adj}}}.
\end{equation}
For ablations and unseen-workload comparisons, we also report improvement versus greedy:
\begin{equation}
\text{Improvement vs Greedy} = \frac{L_{\text{greedy}} - L_{\text{method}}}{L_{\text{greedy}}}.
\end{equation}
Positive values are better for the second metric.

\subsection{Main Result: RL-WS Extends Greedy}

Table~\ref{tab:main_result} shows the workload-level mean latency saving versus adjacent-uniform no-express baselines. Greedy already captures substantial benefit, but RL-WS raises the mean saving for every workload. The uplift is largest on Hybrid TP+PP and Uniform Random, where greedy leaves several competing non-local bottlenecks unresolved. Across all 40 configurations, RL-WS beats adjacent-uniform in every case and improves on greedy in 32/40, ties in 5, and loses in only 3; all three losses are within 1.7\% before fallback.

\section{Discussion}
% ============================================================

\textbf{When is greedy enough?} For lower-NL\% workloads such as Tree All-Reduce, greedy already captures most of the available benefit and RL headroom is small. For higher-NL\% workloads, especially those with many competing long-distance pairs, RL-WS extracts additional gain by revisiting earlier greedy choices.

\textbf{Design-time trade-off.} Greedy runs in seconds and is the right default. The zero-shot GNN is fastest (tens of milliseconds) but only safe in-distribution. RL-WS takes tens to hundreds of seconds per configuration, so it is best suited to offline design-space exploration on fixed workloads.

\textbf{Limitations.} Our workloads are synthetic but workload-inspired rather than traced from full training runs; all results are simulator-based; and we do not provide a formal deadlock-freedom proof for the combined routing policy. These are important next steps before deployment-level claims.

% ============================================================
\section{Conclusion}
% ============================================================

\textbf{Conclusion.} Phantom load is a structural cost problem in chiplet NoI: under adjacent-only routing, center-link amplification grows as $\Theta(K^{3/2})$ and forces costly overprovisioning. Express links attack that problem directly, and the workload's non-locality fraction predicts how much they help. Building on this insight, RL-WS warm-starts from greedy placement, refines it through swap-based RL, and uses post-hoc BookSim fallback to guarantee no regression versus greedy. Across 40 evaluated configurations, greedy achieves 25.6\% mean latency saving over adjacent-uniform no-express baselines, while RL-WS improves that to 28.1\% and reaches 56.4\% in the best case. On unseen workloads, RL-WS remains robust while zero-shot GNN degrades on all-to-all traffic. Together, these results suggest a practical workflow for architects: compute NL\% first, use greedy as the baseline, and invoke warm-start RL when high non-locality leaves meaningful headroom beyond greedy placement.
