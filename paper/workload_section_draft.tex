\subsection{Workloads}
\label{subsec:workloads}

We evaluate four communication patterns that represent the dominant
inter-chiplet traffic regimes observed in current and projected
chiplet-based LLM training/inference systems
(AMD MI300X~\cite{mi300x}, NVIDIA Blackwell~\cite{blackwell}).
The four workloads span an order of magnitude in non-locality fraction
(NL\%) and three structurally distinct dispatch shapes:
butterfly (Tree), grouped-dense (Hybrid TP+PP),
dense-random (Uniform), and skewed-hotspot (MoE).
Fig.~\ref{fig:wl_patterns} shows the chiplet-level connectivity for each
on a $K{=}16$ ($4\times 4$) grid; edge thickness encodes traffic volume
and chiplet shading encodes incoming traffic intensity.

\textbf{Tree All-Reduce (NL\%=42).}
Recursive halving-doubling gradient synchronization
\cite{thakur2005,nccl}, the dominant pattern under data-parallel
training. At each of $\lceil\log_2 K\rceil$ levels, chiplet $i$
exchanges a partial gradient with partner $i \oplus 2^\ell$, halving
the message size each level. The pattern is butterfly-structured: most
exchanges occur at short stride, so adjacent links carry the bulk of
traffic. This is the canonical \textit{low-NL} regime.

\textbf{Hybrid TP+PP (NL\%=77).}
Megatron-LM~\cite{megatron,megatron3} tensor-parallel groups of size
$\text{TP}{=}8$ exchange activations densely within a group; consecutive
groups are connected by a single inter-group pipeline edge. For
$K{=}16$ this realizes two TP groups linked by one PP edge; for
$K{=}32$, four groups linked sequentially. Intra-group traffic is
dense and short, while the single inter-group link concentrates
PP traffic on one chiplet pair.

\textbf{Uniform Random (NL\%=89).}
A synthetic worst-case baseline in which every chiplet pair $(i,j)$
receives independent random traffic weight. Unlike the previous
patterns this is not drawn from a production workload; we include it
because, under the canonical row-major chiplet mapping, the all-pair
dense pattern places approximately $1{-}O(1/K)$ of total demand on
non-adjacent pairs and is therefore a structural worst case for
adjacent-only allocators. It also matches the assumption that
prior PARL~\cite{parl} and Kite~\cite{kite} evaluations made implicitly
when reporting average-case latency across heterogeneous demand.

\textbf{MoE Expert (NL\%=91).}
Top-2 expert dispatch under Zipfian expert-popularity skew. Each
chiplet samples 2 of $K{-}1$ candidate experts with probability
$\Pr(\text{rank } r) \propto 1/r^{1.5}$ and sends activations to
those expert chiplets. The Zipf exponent $s{=}1.5$ matches measured
expert-popularity skew in production MoE language models---DeepSeek-V3
\cite{deepseekv3} reports concentration ratios in the same regime and
Mixtral-8x7B~\cite{mixtral} shows comparable hot-expert dominance
under top-2 routing. Earlier MoE studies that assumed
\textit{uniform} top-$k$ dispatch~\cite{gshard} conflate placement
with load-balancing; the Zipf model isolates the placement signal
that NoI optimization can exploit.

\paragraph{Relevance to current chiplet NoI.}
The four patterns map onto current and projected chiplet AI systems:
TP+PP is the dominant training regime on multi-chiplet GPU clusters
(MI300X, B200); Tree All-Reduce drives the gradient synchronization
step of every distributed-data-parallel workload (NCCL/RCCL ring/tree
collectives); MoE Expert is exercised by every production-scale MoE
model (DeepSeek-V3, Mixtral, GPT-4 class). Uniform Random is a
synthetic baseline retained from prior NoI literature
\cite{kite,florets,parl} for comparability. Forward compatibility:
while current shipping chiplet products have $K{\le}8$~\cite{mi300x},
industry roadmaps project $K{\in}\{16,32\}$ within the next
generation, motivating our evaluation range.
