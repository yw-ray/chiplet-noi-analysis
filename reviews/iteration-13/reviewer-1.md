# Review — Reviewer 1 (Architecture Expert), Iteration 13

## Summary
The paper formulates express-link placement on a 2.5D chiplet interposer as a Predict–Place–Refine workflow: NL% on the demand matrix predicts both raw express benefit and the headroom for learned placement, while a multi-warm-start REINFORCE policy (seeded from greedy and a Flattened-Butterfly allocator, validated post-hoc by BookSim) refines a deterministic baseline. The pivot to "NL% as deployment classifier; RL-WS strictly beats FBfly on high-NL cells, matches on low-NL" is a sharper story than earlier drafts, and the $\Theta(K^{3/2})$ phantom-load bound plus routing-independence experiment give the topological motivation real teeth. However, the architecture-level evaluation is still substantially under-specified: the express-link distance cap, beachfront/PHY budgeting, and CoWoS layer-count modeling are essentially asserted rather than instrumented, and the head-to-head against PARL — the closest prior work and the paper's only learned-placement competitor — is explicitly deferred, which is a serious gap for a top-tier architecture venue.

## Strengths
1. **[S1] The structural argument is real and well-presented.** Section 3 (especially Theorem 1 / Corollary 1 with the $\Theta(K^{3/2})$ scaling, lines 133–148) and the routing-independence study in Table 2 (lines 177–197) together do something most chiplet-NoI papers miss: they decouple the *topological* tax of multi-hop adjacency from any specific routing policy. The XY/YX/ECMP/Valiant comparison directly answers the obvious "smarter routing solves it" pushback, and the closed-form max-amplification example on the 4×4 grid (line 151, $\alpha=16\times$) makes the bound easy to sanity-check.
2. **[S2] NL% as a single static, simulation-free predictor is the right level of abstraction for an architect.** Defining NL% on the *pre-routing* demand matrix (Eq. on lines 207–210) and showing it correlates at $\rho=0.825$ with measured saving across 28 cells is exactly the kind of design-time knob a chiplet planner can compute from a workload trace before standing up BookSim. The sharper claim — that NL% predicts the *FBfly–RL margin*, not just absolute saving — is novel relative to prior NoI-synthesis work and cleanly motivates the deployment-classifier framing.
3. **[S3] The multi-warm-start design with post-hoc BookSim selection is a defensible engineering choice.** Seeding 8 runs from greedy and 8 from FBfly (line 254), keeping the top-3 surrogate-best per run, and then *measuring* all 48 candidates plus the three baselines in BookSim (line 260) means surrogate error cannot silently regress the headline numbers — this is the right answer to the obvious "why trust an MLP surrogate?" objection, and PARL-style cold-start RL does not have this property.

## Weaknesses
1. **[W1] The express-link physical model is too thin for an architecture venue.** Table 8 (lines 376–391) lists wire length / area / power / latency for $d{=}1\!\dots\!4$ but the numbers are asserted ("CoWoS-class estimates") with no derivation, no citation to a wire-delay model (Bakoglu, ITRS, recent CoWoS-S/L characterization), no driver/repeater assumption, and no via penalty even though Section 3.1 (lines 96–105) explicitly says express links may traverse vias across layers. More critically, **the maximum express distance $D$ is never physically motivated**: line 222 just says "Manhattan distance $d \in [2,D]$" and Table 8 stops at $d{=}4$, but the experiments use $K{=}32$ grids where Manhattan distances reach 9. Either the placer is silently capping $D$ (and that cap should be reported per cell with a justification — beachfront-slot count, max wire length within reticle, layer-count budget) or it is not, in which case the cost table is inconsistent with the search space. This is the single most important fix for an architecture submission.
2. **[W2] No head-to-head against PARL, despite PARL being explicitly named as the closest prior work.** The paper acknowledges this (lines 71, 439 limitation iv... actually iii) and defers to "qualitative" positioning in Table 1. For a workshop paper this might be acceptable; for ISCA/MICRO/HPCA/DAC it is not. PARL's reward is multi-objective (interference score) and the workloads differ, but the *placement decisions themselves* — which non-adjacent pairs receive express links under a budget — are directly comparable on the 28 cells here, and the surrogate machinery means the authors already have a fast evaluator. Without a numerical PARL bar in Table 6, the central claim "RL-WS strictly beats the strongest learned baseline" is unsupported; FBfly is a *heuristic* baseline, not the strongest learned placer.
3. **[W3] The architecture-level cost accounting is workload-blind in exactly the place it should not be.** Section 5.3 (lines 372–391) frames RL-WS as "inheriting the same per-link overhead as greedy" because both consume the same total link budget. But the *distance distribution* of the chosen express links matters enormously for interposer area: an RL-WS solution that concentrates capacity on long $d{=}5$+ pairs (the MoE skewed mechanism described on line 364) costs substantially more interposer wire and beachfront PHY than an FBfly solution that spreads links over $d{=}2$ row/column shortcuts, even at identical budget count. The paper never reports the per-method distribution of express-link distances, total wire-mm$^2$, or PHY count, so the "$-83.2\%$ latency vs FBfly" headline cannot be converted into a fair cost-per-performance ratio. An architecture reviewer will want a Pareto plot in (wire-mm$^2$, latency) space, not just (link-count, latency).

## Questions for Authors
1. **[Q1]** What is the maximum express distance $D$ in each of the 28 cells, and how is it set? Is it a fixed constant, a function of $\sqrt{K}$, or a per-cell beachfront/PHY budget? Please report (a) $D$ used per cell, (b) the distribution over $d$ of express links chosen by greedy, FBfly, and RL-WS, and (c) the resulting total wire-mm$^2$ per method, so the cost comparison in Table 8 can be tied back to the placements actually used in Table 6.
2. **[Q2]** The deadlock-freedom argument on lines 404–405 says express links inherit the VC class of the dimension whose hop count they reduce. What about express links at distance $d \ge 2$ that move along *both* X and Y (e.g., a diagonal $d{=}3$ link that bypasses 2 X-hops and 1 Y-hop)? Does the placer restrict the express candidate set to axis-aligned pairs, or does it allow diagonals — and if diagonals are allowed, how is the channel-dependency ordering preserved without a third VC class?

## Missing References
- **Kite/Florets co-design context.** Cite Bharadwaj et al. on accurate interposer interconnect modeling beyond the Kite-DAC2020 paper, and Sharma et al.'s follow-up if available — the FBfly framing in Section 4 borrows from this lineage and deserves a fuller positioning.
- **AI-Multicast / NoI for ML.** Yin & Ma, "AI Multi-Cast: Optimizing All-Reduce on Chiplet-Based Architectures" (DAC 2023) and Iff et al., "RapidChiplet" (DATE 2024) directly target chiplet NoI for ML traffic and should be discussed; their absence makes the related-work coverage feel narrow.
- **UCIe 2.0 / CoWoS-L characterization.** Cite a concrete CoWoS-L wire-delay characterization (e.g., TSMC OIP / VLSI Symposium 2023–2024 papers) to back the $2d$-cycle model and the $\lambda$ sensitivity sweep — the assertion "linear wire-delay model... reflects CoWoS-class wire delay scaling" (line 408) currently has no source.
- **Express-channel literature beyond EVCs/CMesh.** Concentration networks (e.g., Balfour CMesh is cited; consider Grot et al. "Express Cube Topologies" ISCA 2009 / Kim et al. "Flattened Butterfly" MICRO 2007) — the FBfly baseline name should cite the original Kim flattened-butterfly paper, which is missing.
- **PARL.** A serious comparison, not just a citation. See [W2].

## Detailed Comments

**Abstract (lines 28–32).** The headline numbers (35.6% mean, 24/28 strict wins, $-83.2\%$ peak) are well-stated, but "exceeding... a Flattened-Butterfly (FBfly) topology-aware allocator that is the strongest hand-engineered baseline" sets up FBfly as the strongest baseline overall. This is true only against hand-engineered baselines; PARL is the strongest *learned* baseline and the abstract should not elide that distinction.

**Introduction (lines 35–58).** Fig. 1 is well-placed and the diminishing-returns claim ("at $K$=16, 4× budget gives only 1.5× speedup", line 43) is concrete. The four-bullet contribution list (lines 53–58) is clean. One presentation issue: C1 starts with "Predict: NL\%..." but the supporting Spearman number ($\rho=0.83$) is inside the paragraph, not in a callout — consider promoting it to a numbered claim like the FBfly-margin number.

**Section 3.1 (lines 96–105).** The interposer cross-section figure caption is accurate and refreshingly concrete about CoWoS layer count (3–6). However the same paragraph says express links "may transition between metal layers through vias" — this is the right physical observation, but Section 5.3 then ignores via penalty entirely. Either model it (additional ps per via, area for via stacks) or explicitly note in Table 8 that via overhead is not included.

**Theorem 1 / Corollary 1 (lines 133–148).** The result is correct and the proof sketch is implicit in the flow-counting formula. For a venue like ISCA, the reviewer would expect either a one-paragraph proof in an appendix or a citation to a known result if this is folklore. As stated it reads as if the closed form is novel; please clarify.

**Table 2, routing study (lines 177–197).** Useful experiment, but the caveat on line 175 ("not directly comparable to the undirected closed-form values") is buried — readers will compare 16 (Table 1, $K{=}16$) to 111 (Table 2, $K{=}16$) and be confused. A footnote inside the table itself, or an additional column showing the directional version of the Table 1 prediction, would prevent this.

**Section 4, NL% (lines 205–214).** The $\rho=0.825$ pooled correlation is solid, but Fig. 3 caption (line 346) reports "$\rho=0.74$" for the same scatter — these two numbers should match or the paper should explain why they differ (subset? weighting?). Currently this is an internal inconsistency that a careful reviewer will flag.

**Section 5.2, RL-WS design (lines 249–262).** The 501-dim input is well-described. One architectural concern: the surrogate is trained on 1408 samples spanning all 28 cells, which means train/test of the surrogate overlaps with the cells used at evaluation time. This is fine for a *surrogate* whose role is to seed the BookSim shortlist (since the final number is BookSim-measured), but the paper should state this generalization caveat explicitly — otherwise a reader expects standard ML held-out evaluation.

**Section 6.1, Table 6 (lines 311–341).** The 24/28 strict-win count is the headline. Two presentation issues: (a) the "vs FBfly" column mixes win-counts and percentage in the same cell, which is hard to scan — split into two columns. (b) Tree AR row says "2/4 (1t,1L), $-0.8\%$" (line 334) — please define t/L (tie/loss) in the caption.

**Section 6.2, per-cell analysis (lines 359–370).** Excellent — this is exactly the granular discussion an architecture reviewer wants. The mechanistic explanation ("FBfly's symmetric row/column allocation under-provisions the few high-traffic expert pairs in skewed top-2 dispatch") is clear and falsifiable. Strengthens the claim significantly.

**Section 6.3, physical overhead (lines 372–391).** See [W1], [W3]. This is the weakest section relative to venue expectations.

**Section 7, deadlock argument (lines 404–405).** See [Q2]. Either prove it or restrict the candidate set; "outside the scope of this paper" is not adequate for a deadlock claim in an architecture paper.

**Section 7, $\lambda$-sensitivity (lines 407–437).** Genuinely useful — this preempts the "your savings are an artifact of the $2d$-cycle model" objection cleanly. Worth keeping.

**Conclusion (lines 442–445).** Reasonable. The "compute NL% first, place with FBfly, invoke RL-WS when NL\%$\ge$77\%" workflow is a clear takeaway.

## Rating
- Novelty: 3 / 5 (NL% as deployment classifier is novel; multi-warm-start + post-hoc BookSim selection is a sound but incremental engineering combination)
- Technical Quality: 3 / 5 ($\Theta(K^{3/2})$ + routing study + 28-cell sweep are solid; physical modeling and PARL comparison are weak)
- Significance: 3 / 5 (chiplet NoI for LLMs is timely; $-83\%$ MoE latency is a real result; impact bounded by missing PARL bar and physical-cost accounting)
- Presentation: 4 / 5 (clean Predict–Place–Refine framing, well-organized tables, two minor inconsistencies — $\rho$ value, t/L key)
- Overall: 3 / 5
- Confidence: 4 / 5

## Decision
Borderline (lean Weak Reject without the PARL bar and a tightened express-distance / wire-cost accounting; would move to Weak Accept with both fixed in revision).
