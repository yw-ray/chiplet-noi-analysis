# Review — Reviewer 2 (Systems Pragmatist), Iteration 13

## Summary

The paper proposes a Predict--Place--Refine workflow for express-link placement in 2.5D chiplet NoIs. It claims that a single static statistic, the non-locality fraction NL%, can act as a deployment classifier that decides whether a workload-blind topology-aware allocator (FBfly) is sufficient or whether a multi-warm-start RL refinement (RL-WS) over a rate-aware MLP surrogate is worth invoking. The headline numbers (35.6% mean latency saving over adjacent-uniform across 28 cells, 24/28 strict wins over FBfly, $-83.2\%$ on MoE Skewed $K{=}32, N{=}8$) are evaluated entirely in BookSim 2.0 with synthetic workload-inspired traffic matrices and a linear $2d$-cycle wire-delay model.

## Strengths

1. **Honest, well-stratified result presentation.** The High-NL vs Low-NL stratification, the explicit acknowledgment that FBfly already wins on 12/28 cells, and the per-cell breakdown in Section 5.3 (only the 3 MoE Skewed cells drive most of the $-11.8\%$ FBfly margin; "outside MoE, the typical reduction is 3--5\%") is the kind of disclosure I rarely see in DAC/ISCA submissions and it materially raises my trust. The authors have not buried the lede.
2. **The deployment-classifier framing is genuinely useful.** "Compute NL% first, run FBfly if NL$\le$50%, only invoke RL when NL$\ge$77% AND the traffic is skewed" is a deployable rule of thumb; it tells an architect what to *do*, not just what the authors measured. The $\lambda$-sensitivity sweep (Table 8) further argues the express-link benefit is not an artifact of an optimistic 2$d$-cycle wire model.
3. **The Place baselines are strong.** Adding FBfly as a no-RL topology-aware baseline (in addition to greedy) is the right move. It dissolves the obvious "your RL beats a weak greedy heuristic" objection and forces the RL contribution to be measured against the strongest hand-engineered alternative.

## Weaknesses

1. **No real hardware, no real LLM trace --- not even a reduced FPGA emulation or a profiler trace from a published MoE inference run.** The entire 28-cell evaluation is BookSim 2.0 with synthetic traffic matrices (tree, ring, hybrid TP+PP, MoE Skewed, all-to-all, uniform random, pipeline). For a paper that makes a *deployability* claim --- "use this workflow at design time for your chiplet NoI" --- the absence of any cross-validation against either (a) a real MoE inference trace from Mixtral / DeepSeek-MoE / GShard, (b) a Megatron-LM TP+PP trace, or (c) at minimum a software model whose injection rates and burst patterns were calibrated to a published profiler dump, is the central deployability gap. The MoE Skewed top-2 dispatch is described as "skewed" but the paper never tells me *what skew distribution* (Zipf with what exponent? based on whose published expert-routing histogram?), which makes the headline $-83.2\%$ MoE Skewed number very hard to interpret. If the assumed skew is more extreme than measured Mixtral-8x7B routing skew, the headline number is unreproducible at deploy time.
2. **The end-to-end design-time cost of the proposed workflow is undisclosed and probably the dominant adoption barrier.** Section 6 reports "RL-WS takes minutes per configuration (1000 episodes $\times$ 16 warm-start seeds plus BookSim selection over $\le$48 candidates)". This is missing all the cost that a deployer actually pays: (i) the surrogate was trained on **1408 BookSim samples spanning all 28 evaluated cells** (Section 5.2: "trained on 1408 BookSim samples that span all evaluated $(\text{workload}, K, N, b)$ cells") --- so on a *new* workload-config cell the surrogate is by construction in-distribution because it was trained on that very cell's samples. The paper does not separate "amortized surrogate training over many cells" from "per-cell deploy-time RL+BookSim refinement", and does not run a leave-one-cell-out generalization test for the surrogate. (ii) BookSim selection over $\le$48 candidates per cell at four injection rates is $\le 192$ BookSim runs per cell; on a 28-cell sweep that's $\approx 5400$ BookSim invocations *just for final selection*, on top of the 1408 training samples. The wall-clock is probably not "minutes" once you include surrogate training. (iii) On a brand-new workload class (say a future multimodal model with a routing pattern outside the seven trained ones), is the surrogate still useful, or does it need retraining? The paper does not say.
3. **NL% as a "static workload property" needs deployer-side legwork the paper hides.** NL% is computed on the pre-routing demand matrix $T$. To compute it at design time, an architect must (a) obtain a representative $T$ for the workload, (b) commit to a chiplet-level grid layout (NL% changes if you change which physical chiplet hosts which logical role), and (c) decide what the threshold (50% / 77%) means under expected workload mix shift over the chip's lifetime. The paper presents NL% as cheap ("a single static statistic") without quantifying the upstream cost of obtaining $T$. For training-only chiplets that's manageable; for inference accelerators servicing mixed model traffic in production it is not, because the deployed traffic mix drifts. A deployability-focused paper should at least sketch how NL% behaves under workload mixing (e.g., 60% MoE + 40% TP traffic --- is that the weighted average NL%? Is it still predictive of RL-WS benefit, or does mixing hide the skew that drives the MoE win?).

## Questions for Authors

1. **Real-trace validation.** Can you cross-validate even one of your seven workloads against a published profiler trace? For MoE Skewed specifically: which expert-routing skew (Zipf exponent? empirical histogram from which model?) is your traffic matrix calibrated to? If I plug in the measured Mixtral-8x7B routing distribution, do I still see $-83.2\%$ saving or does the gap collapse?
2. **Surrogate generalization.** What is the surrogate's held-out Spearman $\rho$ when you do *leave-one-workload-out* (train on 6 workloads, test on the 7th) instead of the random 80/20 split reported (val MSE 1924, $\rho{=}0.928$)? An 80/20 random split with samples from the same 28 cells in both sets does not measure the cost a deployer would pay on a new workload, and the published $\rho{=}0.928$ is therefore optimistic. The same question applies leave-one-$(K,N)$-out: if I deploy at $K{=}64$, does the surrogate still reward-shape correctly?
3. **End-to-end wall-clock at deploy time.** For one representative high-NL cell (say MoE Skewed $K{=}32, N{=}8, b{=}4\times$), please report the actual wall-clock for: (a) generating the 1408 BookSim training samples, (b) training the rate-aware MLP, (c) running the 16 warm-start REINFORCE seeds for 1000 episodes each, (d) BookSim-validating the 48 surrogate-best candidates plus 3 baselines at 4 rates each. The "minutes per configuration" claim in Section 6 is not credible at face value if (a) is included.
4. **Multi-run RL variance.** Limitation (ii) admits "we do not report per-cell BookSim variance over independent end-to-end RL re-runs". For the three MoE Skewed cells that drive the headline gap, please report mean and std of final RL-WS BookSim latency over (say) 5 independent end-to-end re-runs (different RNG for the 16 seeds). If the std is 5--10\% of the mean, the $-83.2\%$ headline becomes "$-78\pm 5$%" which is still impressive but should be reported honestly.
5. **NL% under workload mixing.** If the deployed traffic is a weighted mixture of, e.g., 60% MoE Skewed + 40% Hybrid TP+PP, does the NL% of the mixture still predict express-link saving with similar Spearman $\rho$? Or does the deployment classifier need to be re-derived per traffic-mixing scenario?
6. **FBfly per-pair cap interaction.** Section 5.1 defines FBfly with a per-pair cap of $N$; Table 6 shows MoE Skewed at $K{=}32, N{=}8, b{=}4\times$ saturates at 402.8 cycles for FBfly. Is the saturation because FBfly hits the per-pair cap on its hot rows/columns, or because the row/column-regular allocation simply does not match the skewed expert-pair distribution at all? An ablation that loosens the per-pair cap on FBfly would clarify whether the $-83.2\%$ gap is fundamentally about the regularity prior, or only about a cap parameter that a smarter heuristic could relax.
7. **Deadlock claim.** Section 6 says "A formal Duato-style proof is outside the scope of this paper". For a paper that wants to be deployed, the absence of a formal proof for the proposed VC assignment (one VC per dimension, express link assigned to its dimension's VC class) is uncomfortable. At minimum, can you exhibit a BookSim simulation under adversarial traffic showing no deadlock for several billion cycles on the largest configuration? Or cite a closely related construction whose Duato proof transfers?

## Missing References

- **A real MoE expert-routing trace.** Citing measured expert-load skew from a published MoE model (Mixtral 8x7B, DeepSeek-MoE, GShard, Switch Transformer) would let the reader judge whether your "MoE Skewed" matches reality. As written, "skewed top-2 dispatch" is unanchored.
- **CPElide [9] is in your bibliography but never cited in the body.** It is listed in the references but I could not find a `\cite{cpelide}` in the text. Either cite it (multi-chiplet GPU synchronization is genuinely relevant to the deadlock-freedom paragraph) or remove it.
- **CATCH [8] and Chiplet Actuary [7]** are also in the bibliography but never cited in the body. Same comment.
- **A real-hardware chiplet measurement paper.** Even a citation acknowledging that no commercial chiplet product currently exposes the express-link primitive in question (UCIe 1.x/2.0 is point-to-point per the spec, as your Section 3 already notes) would help anchor the deployability discussion. Right now the paper claims a deployment workflow without naming any concrete deployment target.
- **NoI workload trace work.** If any prior chiplet-NoI paper has published recorded traces, citing it would strengthen the synthetic-vs-real disclosure in Limitation (i).

## Detailed Comments

**Abstract (lines 28--32).** "$-83.2\%$ latency vs FBfly" in the abstract should be flagged in-text as a single-cell peak driven by MoE Skewed, not a representative number. The pooled mean over FBfly is $-11.8\%$ (line 338, Table 6 last row); leading the abstract with the cell maximum without that context is a small overclaim. Section 5.3 (lines 362--368) does the right thing internally ("These three cells alone contribute most of the $-11.8\%$") but the abstract reader does not see that.

**Section 1, lines 38--39.** "industry roadmaps project 16--64 chiplets in next-generation designs" needs a citation. Without one this reads as speculation; with one (even a public roadmap deck) it grounds the $K\!\in\!\{16,32\}$ choice as more than a convenient simulator setting.

**Section 4, lines 213--214.** The Spearman $\rho{=}0.825$ across 28 cells is the load-bearing predictor result. The pool mixes $K{=}16$ and $K{=}32$ cells of the same workload --- so the rank correlation is partly capturing "$K{=}32$ has more phantom load and therefore more headroom" rather than "NL% of one workload predicts saving for that workload". A within-$(K,N)$-strata Spearman (i.e. compute $\rho$ separately within each of the four $(K,N)$ panels, then report the four $\rho$ values plus a combined-strata-controlled estimate) would isolate the workload-NL% predictor from the configuration-driven trend. The current pooled $\rho$ is the optimistic version of this number.

**Section 5.1, line 270.** "$b{=}2\times$ for budget-frontier comparability" is fine, but Table 6 mixes a $b{=}2\times$ cell (Tree, $K{=}32, N{=}8$) into a column otherwise reporting $b{=}4\times$ savings. The "primarily $b{=}4\times$" caveat in the table caption is doing a lot of work; please mark the $b{=}2\times$ cell explicitly in Table 6 (footnote or asterisk) so a reader scanning the table does not silently compare across budgets.

**Section 5.2, line 256.** "trained on 1408 BookSim samples that span all evaluated $(\text{workload}, K, N, b)$ cells". This sentence is the deployability problem in one line. If the surrogate is trained on samples drawn from the very 28 cells it is later used to evaluate, the surrogate is being graded on its in-distribution behavior, and a deployer evaluating a new workload-config cell receives no guarantee about reward-shaping quality. Please run leave-one-cell-out (or leave-one-workload-out) and report both the surrogate's predictive Spearman $\rho$ and the resulting end-to-end RL-WS BookSim latency.

**Section 5.3, lines 362--368.** This is the most honest paragraph in the paper and I want to commend it. Please consider promoting the "MoE-only" caveat into the abstract / introduction so the reader does not have to wait until Section 5.3 to learn that the headline mean-FBfly-margin is approximately three cells, not 28.

**Section 6, line 399.** "Greedy and FBfly run in seconds and form strong deterministic baselines. RL-WS takes minutes per configuration". The "minutes per configuration" claim is not substantiated --- no wall-clock breakdown table exists. For a deployability paper, a Table that lists `Method | Wall-clock | Hardware | Reproducible?` (e.g., "Greedy: 2.3 s on 1 CPU core; FBfly: 0.8 s; RL-WS: X min on Y GPU including Z BookSim runs") would be more persuasive than the current prose.

**Section 6, lines 404--405.** Deadlock-freedom paragraph: "A formal Duato-style proof is outside the scope of this paper, but the above construction requires no new routing primitives beyond those already present in BookSim's anynet topology support." For deploy-time use, "BookSim has the feature" is necessary but not sufficient. Please at minimum confirm that none of your 28 RL-WS allocations triggers BookSim's deadlock detector under saturating injection.

**Limitation (i), line 439.** "synthetic but workload-inspired rather than traced from full training runs" --- this is the right thing to say. I would push the authors to go one step further and quantify the calibration: e.g., "MoE Skewed uses Zipf(s=$\alpha$) over $E$ experts; setting $\alpha={\cdot}$ matches the routing histogram reported in [cite]." Without that calibration line, the synthetic-trace caveat is honest but does not give the deployer a way to know whether their workload looks like your MoE Skewed.

## Rating

- Novelty: 3 / 5 (NL% as classifier + dual warm-start + rate-aware surrogate is a sensible composition; each piece is incremental over prior work but the *workflow* framing is novel.)
- Technical Quality: 3 / 5 (BookSim-only; surrogate evaluated only on in-distribution split; no multi-run variance; no formal deadlock proof.)
- Significance: 3 / 5 (Deployment-classifier framing is useful, but the $-83.2\%$ headline rests on three MoE cells whose skew calibration is unspecified.)
- Presentation: 4 / 5 (Stratified High-NL/Low-NL framing is clear; honest per-cell disclosure in 5.3 is unusual and welcome; some bibliographic deadweight (CPElide, CATCH, Actuary uncited).)
- Overall: 3 / 5
- Confidence: 4 / 5

## Decision

**Borderline (leaning Weak Reject).** The paper's deployment-classifier framing is genuinely useful, the FBfly baseline is the right strengthening move, and Section 5.3's per-cell honesty is rare. But for a paper whose central pitch is *deployability*, the all-BookSim, all-synthetic-traffic evaluation, the surrogate trained on the exact cells it is later graded on, the missing wall-clock cost breakdown, the unspecified MoE skew calibration, and the absence of any cross-validation against a real LLM trace add up to a meaningful gap. I would readily flip to Weak Accept if the authors (a) calibrate MoE Skewed to a cited expert-routing histogram, (b) add a leave-one-workload-out surrogate evaluation, and (c) provide an end-to-end wall-clock table including surrogate training. Without those, the paper is one revision short of a deployability story I can recommend.
