# Reviewer 3 (ML / LLM Application Expert) — Iteration 13

**Paper:** Predict, Place, Refine: Non-Locality-Guided Express Link Placement for LLM Chiplet Networks

## Summary

The paper proposes a three-step recipe (Predict / Place / Refine) for express-link placement on a 2.5D chiplet network-on-interposer. A static traffic statistic, the non-locality fraction NL%, is used to (i) predict the absolute benefit of express links and (ii) classify when a learned placer (RL-WS) is worth running over a topology-aware regular allocator (FBfly). RL-WS is REINFORCE with 16 warm-start seeds (8 from greedy, 8 from FBfly), trained against a rate-aware MLP surrogate, with the top-3 surrogate-predicted candidates per seed re-validated in BookSim. The headline numbers are +35.6% mean latency saving vs adjacent-uniform across 28 cells, strict wins on 24/28 vs FBfly, and a peak −83.2% on MoE Skewed K=32, N=8, b=4×.

I am Reviewer 3 with an LLM-systems background (Megatron-LM, NCCL, MoE dispatch). I evaluate the paper primarily on whether the workloads represent real LLM training/inference, whether NL% is a meaningful workload property at the level claimed, and whether the gains translate into anything a model-systems team would care about.

## Strengths

- **S1. Genuinely useful framing for systems folks.** "Compute one scalar (NL%) on the demand matrix and decide whether to bother with a learned placer" is the kind of operational hook ML-systems readers respond to. The high-NL vs low-NL stratification (Table 6, lines 320–341) is presented cleanly and the deployment-classifier story is internally consistent.
- **S2. Workload palette is broader than most NoI papers.** Many prior NoI works evaluate on synthetic uniform/transpose/hotspot only. Here we get Tree AR, Ring AR, Pipeline, Hybrid TP+PP (with TP=8), MoE skewed top-2, All-to-all, and Uniform Random — covering a meaningful slice of the AllReduce / TP / PP / MoE space (lines 274–293).
- **S3. Skewed MoE with Zipf is the right call.** Switching from uniform top-2 to Zipf(s=1.5) (per `gen_moe` in `cost_perf_6panel_workload.py`) is a strict improvement and gestures at the actual hot-expert behavior reported in DeepSeek-V3, Mixtral, and OLMoE. The MoE K=32, N=8, b=4× cell shows exactly the regime where MoE-aware placement matters most.
- **S4. Surrogate has a real held-out validation number.** Spearman ρ=0.928 on a 20% split, plus the explicit comparison to a rate-blind v1 (ρ=0.902, line 256) — that's the right level of due diligence on the reward proxy.
- **S5. Honest about negatives.** The paper does not hide the 4/28 non-strict-wins and explicitly justifies the low-NL ties as the very point of the deployment-classifier story (lines 370–371). The λ-sensitivity sweep (Table 9, lines 410–437) is a nice robustness check that ML/architecture readers will appreciate.

## Weaknesses

- **W1. NL% is treated as a workload property but it is actually a *workload × placement* property.** This is my biggest issue. NL% as defined in Eq. (5) (lines 206–210) depends on the chiplet graph G — i.e., on which logical rank lands on which physical chiplet. A Hybrid TP+PP run with TP=8 has NL%≈77% only under the *specific* mapping where ranks 0..7 are scattered across non-adjacent chiplets. If a sane systems engineer co-locates the TP group on adjacent chiplets (which is exactly what NCCL's default ring builder + topology-aware NCCL_TOPO_FILE *does*), NL% drops dramatically and the entire premise of "this workload needs RL-WS" disappears. The paper never specifies the rank→chiplet mapping for any workload. Without that, the per-workload NL% numbers in Table 4 (line 286) are not reproducible and the deployment-classifier claim cannot be operationalized. **This needs to be fixed**: either (a) report NL% under a stated and defensible mapping (e.g., "Megatron's default TP-major linear layout") and acknowledge that NL% is a function of layout, or (b) jointly optimize layout and express placement, in which case the express-saving numbers should be reported relative to the *optimized-layout* baseline, not the worst-case linear layout.

- **W2. Generalization-to-unseen-workloads claim is not actually supported.** The CLAUDE.md notes claim "RL-WS wins 6/6 on UNSEEN workloads (ring, pipeline, all-to-all)", and the paper's own line 303 says "we also report relative improvement versus greedy ... For ablations and unseen-workload comparisons". But line 256 explicitly states the surrogate is trained on 1408 samples that "span all evaluated (workload, K, N, b) cells". So Ring, Pipeline, and All-to-all were in the training set — they are not held out in any meaningful ML sense. This is a significant overstatement that any reviewer with ML training will catch immediately. Either (a) retrain the surrogate with a leave-one-workload-out protocol and re-report, or (b) drop the unseen-workload framing entirely and call it what it is: in-distribution evaluation across 28 cells.

- **W3. The headline −83% MoE result rests on one cell and one mechanism.** Section 5.3 (lines 359–368) is admirably honest that 3 of the 28 cells (all MoE Skewed) drive most of the −11.8% mean reduction vs FBfly, and outside MoE the typical reduction is 3–5%. The mechanism is also clear: FBfly cannot exploit Zipf hotness. But this is precisely the regime where a *software* fix is straightforward — expert co-location / token-level expert load balancing (DeepSpeed-MoE, Tutel, and the recent expert-parallel work in DeepSeek-V3 all do this). A skeptical reader will ask: "Why am I solving Zipf hotness with $$ of interposer wires when I can solve it in the dispatcher?" The paper needs a paragraph that engages this directly. As written, the MoE case looks like a hardware solution to a software problem.

- **W4. No connection from latency saving to LLM wall-clock throughput.** This is the single biggest gap from an LLM-systems perspective. The paper reports BookSim max-rate latency in cycles, but training/inference users care about tokens/sec, time-per-iteration, or step time. A −83% latency saving on a *single collective* (MoE all-to-all) does not translate to −83% step time, because (a) compute and communication overlap, (b) AllReduce is bandwidth-bound at large message sizes and the latency-tail regime where express links shine is only a fraction of the per-step communication time, and (c) MoE dispatch is one of several collectives in a Mixtral/DeepSeek step. A back-of-envelope mapping (say, plug the BookSim cycle count into a roofline-style model with a representative compute time) would dramatically strengthen the significance claim. Without it, "+35.6% saving vs adjacent-uniform" is decoupled from any practitioner-relevant metric.

- **W5. Software baseline for AllReduce is missing entirely.** Hierarchical AllReduce (NCCL's default for >8 GPUs), 2D ring AllReduce, and recursive halving-doubling are *exactly* the kind of optimizations that flatten phantom load without hardware changes. The paper's "Tree All-Reduce" workload appears to be recursive halving-doubling (lines 127–139 of `cost_perf_6panel_workload.py`: butterfly XOR pattern), which is already topology-aware. But Ring AllReduce at NL=13% (Table 4, line 290) is the *naive* large-K ring; in practice no one runs Ring on K=32 chiplets — they run hierarchical or 2D ring. So the low-NL workloads benefit less from express links *partly because the implementation is already adjacency-friendly* (Tree/butterfly), but the comparison would look different against naive vs hierarchical AllReduce. A small comparison table — naive ring, 2D ring, recursive halving-doubling — would let the reader see whether express links beat collective-level optimizations or merely substitute for them.

- **W6. Hybrid TP+PP traffic model is questionable.** Looking at `gen_hybrid_tp_pp` (lines 54–69), TP=8 produces dense intra-group all-to-all-like traffic plus a small inter-group bridge edge with weight 0.3. This is closer to "TP all-gather + PP point-to-point" than to a real Megatron-LM step, which has (i) TP all-gather, (ii) TP all-reduce at fwd+bwd, (iii) PP send/recv with a 1F1B schedule, and (iv) optimizer state communication if ZeRO is in use. The volumes are very different across these (TP all-reduce is dominant at large hidden sizes; PP send/recv is small). Treating them as a single dense matrix flattens that structure and likely overstates non-locality. I would not block on this — synthetic LLM workloads always simplify — but the paper should at minimum cite Megatron-LM and acknowledge the simplification.

- **W7. Surrogate generalization across (K, N, b) is not demonstrated.** The 501-dim input includes K, N, log(rate), budget multiplier b (line 256), but with only 1408 training samples spanning 7 workloads × {K=16,32} × {N=4,8} × 4 rates × multiple budgets, each (workload, K, N, b) cell has ~50 samples. The 80/20 split is per-sample, not per-cell, so the validation set almost certainly contains samples from every cell. This means the reported ρ=0.928 measures *interpolation within seen cells*, not generalization to an unseen (K, N) configuration. For the deployment-classifier story to hold at K=64 or N=16 (which the paper teases at Section 3.3), out-of-cell generalization needs to be shown. A leave-one-K-out experiment would be small effort with large payoff.

- **W8. Comparison with PARL is hand-waved.** Section 2 (lines 70–71) and Section 6 limitation (iii) at line 439 both note that PARL is targeting a different objective (multi-tenant interference) and that a head-to-head reproduction is left as future work. That's fair, but the related-work table (Table 1, line 73) places "Ours" against PARL on three axes (Predictor / Warm-start / Surrogate). Without an experimental comparison, the architectural-novelty claim relative to the closest learned-placement work is qualitative. At minimum, run RL-WS on whichever benchmark PARL released (or vice versa) on one shared cell.

## Questions

- **Q1.** What is the rank→chiplet mapping for each workload? Specifically for Hybrid TP+PP at K=32, where do the 4 TP groups of size 8 land on the 4×8 grid? Is the mapping fixed across all 28 cells?
- **Q2.** If you co-locate each TP-8 group on a 2×4 sub-grid (which a topology-aware NCCL build does), what is the resulting NL% and the resulting RL-WS saving?
- **Q3.** Is the surrogate's 80/20 split done per-sample or per-cell? If per-sample, please add a per-cell holdout number.
- **Q4.** For the headline MoE Skewed K=32 N=8 result (67.8 vs 402.8 cycles, line 316), what does the comparison look like against (a) MoE with capacity-factor-based dropping and (b) MoE with random expert placement randomization across 5 seeds? I am trying to understand how much of the gain is "RL placed wires correctly" vs "RL accidentally compensated for the worst-case Zipf realization".
- **Q5.** What injection rate r is used in the Table 6 main-result numbers? Line 297 says "the four training rates" — is the headline saving the max-rate result, or averaged, or something else?
- **Q6.** Can you provide a step-time decomposition for one realistic LLM step (e.g., Mixtral-8x7B training step, batch=4M tokens)? Even a calibrated estimate using BookSim cycles as the comm time would let readers translate −83% MoE-collective latency into a fraction of training step time.
- **Q7.** What budget is used for the "All-to-all" NL=90% workload, and how does its peak saving compare to MoE Skewed at the same NL%? They have nearly identical NL% but very different traffic structures (uniform vs sparse skewed); the paper's per-workload result table (line 330 vs 329) shows All-to-all at +38.4% vs MoE at +86.1%, which itself is evidence that NL% is *not* sufficient as a single-statistic predictor — there is structural information NL% misses.

## Missing References

The paper could substantially strengthen its LLM-application framing with the following:

- **NCCL collective design**: Sanders et al., "Optimal All-Gather Algorithms" (the recursive halving-doubling reference), and the NCCL 2.x topology-aware ring/tree builder. Relevant to W5.
- **Megatron-LM**: Shoeybi et al., "Megatron-LM: Training Multi-Billion Parameter Language Models Using Model Parallelism" (NVIDIA, 2019/2020). The Hybrid TP+PP workload model is unsupported without this.
- **DeepSpeed-MoE / Tutel**: Rajbhandari et al. (DeepSpeed-MoE) and He et al. (Tutel) for the expert-parallel software baseline that addresses Zipf hotness without changing hardware. Relevant to W3.
- **DeepSeek-V3 / Mixtral**: For empirical Zipf-skew justification (the paper's `gen_moe` docstring already mentions DeepSeek-V3 and Mixtral, but these are uncited in the bibliography).
- **GShard**: Lepikhin et al. (the original MoE-at-scale work). Sets the context for "expert dispatch is a real network problem" that this paper is solving.
- **Expert co-location work**: Hwang et al. "Tutel" or the NeurIPS 2024 expert-affinity placement work. Relevant to W3.
- **2D Torus AllReduce / ZeRO++**: Wang et al. "ZeRO++" or the 2D Hierarchical AllReduce literature. Relevant to W5.

## Detailed Comments (with line numbers)

- **L29 (Abstract).** "non-locality fraction (NL%, the share of traffic volume between non-adjacent chiplet pairs)" — please add "*under a given chiplet layout*" so readers immediately understand NL% is layout-dependent. Otherwise the abstract reads as if NL% were a pure workload property, which it is not.
- **L31 (Abstract).** "best single cell reaches −83.2% latency vs FBfly on MoE Skewed K=32, N=8, b=4×". Strong number; please also report the *mean* −19% on high-NL cells with similar prominence, since the −83% is one cell.
- **L208–211 (Eq. 5).** NL% definition is fine, but please add a sentence: "Because hop_G(i,j) depends on the rank-to-chiplet assignment, NL% is a property of the (workload, layout) pair." Then state which layout is used in the rest of the paper.
- **L256 (Surrogate).** "trained on 1408 BookSim samples that span all evaluated (workload, K, N, b) cells". This is the smoking gun for W2. If you keep the unseen-workload language elsewhere, this sentence directly contradicts it.
- **L262.** "Why RL helps." This paragraph is too short. A 2–3 sentence intuition for why multi-warm-start beats single-warm-start would help: "Greedy is optimal on skewed traffic, FBfly is optimal on near-uniform traffic, and the optimum on mixed regimes lies between the two; the RL search can interpolate, but only if it sees both anchors."
- **L270 ("primarily at link-budget b=4×").** The smallest cell uses b=2× for "budget-frontier comparability". This is a bit awkward — a reader will wonder if the small cell would have shown a regression at b=4× and was therefore reported at b=2×. A footnote with the b=4× number for that cell would dispel suspicion.
- **L302 ("Saving vs Adj").** Adjacent-uniform is a weak baseline (no express, uniform allocation). It is fine as a *floor*, but the more meaningful comparator for a systems audience is FBfly or greedy, since adjacent-uniform is not what anyone would actually deploy. Please foreground "RL-WS vs FBfly" earlier than line 314.
- **L309 (Low-budget regime).** Honest disclosure of the b=2× crossover. Good.
- **L316–317.** "FBfly's regular row/column allocation saturates at 402.8 cycles while RL-WS attains 67.8 cycles". A 6× ratio is so dramatic it raises a sanity-check question: does FBfly's 402.8 indicate genuine network saturation under MoE Zipf, or is it an artifact of a per-pair cap interaction? A short paragraph confirming saturation (e.g., showing the most-loaded link's offered load > 1 under FBfly) would close the loop.
- **L329–340 (Table 6).** Strongest single piece of the paper. Please add a column for *peak* (not just mean) saving per workload — readers will want to see that Tree's −0.8% mean vs FBfly is not hiding a −5% worst case.
- **L368 ("This is the empirical answer to ...").** Very strong takeaway sentence. Consider promoting it to the Discussion.
- **L398.** "RL-WS is essential rather than incremental" is slightly overclaimed when the gap on All-to-all (NL=90%) is only −4.2%. The "essential" framing applies to *MoE-style skewed dispatch*, not to all high-NL workloads. Tighten the language.
- **L405 (Deadlock freedom).** "A formal Duato-style proof is outside the scope of this paper" — that is acceptable for the experimental contribution, but Reviewer 1 (architecture) will likely push harder. Consider adding a half-page proof sketch in an appendix or a simulation-based deadlock check (run BookSim under heavy load and report no credit-deadlock observed).
- **L439 (Limitations).** The list is good but missing the layout-dependence limitation flagged in W1. Please add: "(vii) NL% and the resulting placement assume a fixed rank-to-chiplet layout; jointly optimizing layout and express placement is future work."

## Decision

**Weak Reject** for top-tier (ISCA/MICRO/HPCA). Borderline accept for DAC/DATE.

Two issues are blocking for a top-tier venue: (W1) NL% is presented as a workload statistic but is actually a (workload, layout) statistic, which undermines the "single static predictor" framing without a clear caveat and a stated mapping; and (W2) the "unseen workloads" claim is not supported by the surrogate training protocol. Both are fixable in a major revision: (W1) by stating the mapping and showing one alternative-mapping comparison, and (W2) by either dropping the unseen-workload framing or running a leave-one-workload-out protocol (one extra surrogate retrain per held-out workload).

If both are fixed and a back-of-envelope LLM-step decomposition is added (W4), I would move to Weak Accept. The framing is genuinely useful and the MoE result, properly contextualized against software mitigations, is publishable.
