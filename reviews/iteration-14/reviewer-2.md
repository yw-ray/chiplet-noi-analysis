# Review --- Reviewer 2 (Systems Pragmatist), Iteration 14

## Summary

Iter-14 is a disclosure-and-decomposition revision of iter-13. The headline workflow (Predict NL% $\to$ Place via greedy/FBfly $\to$ Refine via multi-warm-start RL with rate-aware surrogate and post-hoc BookSim selection) and the headline numbers are unchanged: 35.6% mean saving vs adjacent-uniform across 28 cells, 24/28 strict wins over FBfly, $-83.2\%$ peak on MoE Skewed $K{=}32, N{=}8, b{=}4\times$, $\rho{=}0.83$. The substantive changes from iter-13 are: (a) the surrogate is now explicitly framed as in-distribution within the random 80/20 split, with the post-hoc BookSim fallback (Eq.~6) acknowledged as the actual safety mechanism rather than surrogate accuracy; (b) the ablation table now decomposes BookSim-best source into 17/28 greedy-warm vs 11/28 FBfly-warm wins; (c) the MoE dependence is now disclosed as a Limitation (ii) bullet ($-3.2\%$ excluding MoE vs $-11.8\%$ with MoE) and reiterated in the Conclusion; (d) the numerical inconsistencies on $\rho$ and on the Greedy/FBfly/RL means I flagged in iter-13 are fixed (Table~6 and abstract now agree at $+24.8\%/+27.1\%/+35.6\%$ and $\rho{=}0.83$).

## Strengths

1. **Honest in-distribution scoping of the surrogate.** Section~5.2 line 257 now reads "the surrogate is a well-matched in-distribution reward model for refinement, not a held-out generalization predictor. We rely on the post-hoc BookSim selection step (Eq.~\ref{eq:fallback}) for the actual safety guarantee, so surrogate generalization error is bounded only as well as it needs to rank candidates within a cell." This is the right reframing of my iter-13 weakness W1: the safety story is the BookSim fallback, *not* surrogate generalization. Limitation (iv) in the Discussion repeats this scoping concisely. I also note that leave-one-workload-out is correctly flagged as future work rather than silently omitted. This is a substantive epistemic upgrade.
2. **Multi-warm-start contribution is now decomposed.** Table~7 (Ablation) reports BookSim-best source = 17/28 greedy-warm + 11/28 FBfly-warm. Combined with the per-workload pattern in Section~5.1 ("greedy stronger on skewed; FBfly stronger on near-uniform"), this is sufficient evidence that *both* warm-starts pull weight. The ``raw RL vs $\min(\text{greedy}, \text{FBfly})$'' sub-block (22 strict beats / 2 ties / 4 losses by $\le$0.9\,cycle) and the $4/28$ fallback activation count complete the picture.
3. **The MoE dependence is now in the Limitations section, not just Section~5.3.** Limitation (ii) at line 487 spells out ``excluding MoE, the per-cell mean reduction across the remaining 24 cells is $-3.2\%$ rather than $-11.8\%$'' and the Conclusion (line 493) repeats this. A reader skimming only the Limitations / Conclusion now cannot miss the MoE-dominance caveat. This is the right call.
4. **Numerical consistency.** Iter-13 had a small drift between abstract/text/Table~6 on the headline saving values ($+24.8/27.1/35.6$ are now consistent). $\rho{=}0.83$ is now used everywhere. The peak cell $-83.2\%$ vs FBfly on MoE $K{=}32, N{=}8, b{=}4\times$ ties cleanly to the per-cell numbers in Section~5.3.

## Weaknesses

1. **The promised explicit per-cell cost breakdown (§VI.C in the iter-14 changelog) is not actually in the paper as a wall-clock table.** The decomposition that *is* present is the BookSim-best source split (17 greedy-warm / 11 FBfly-warm) and the ``$\le$48 BookSim runs per cell'' figure, both of which were qualitatively present in iter-13. What I asked for in iter-13 Q3 was a wall-clock table with rows for (a) generating the 1408 BookSim training samples, (b) training the rate-aware MLP, (c) running the 16 warm-start REINFORCE seeds for 1000 episodes each, and (d) BookSim-validating the 48 candidates plus the 3 baselines at 4 rates each, with (a) explicitly amortized over how many cells. Section~6 still says only ``RL-WS takes minutes per configuration (1000 episodes $\times$ 16 warm-start seeds plus BookSim selection over $\le$48 candidates)'' --- same prose as iter-13. For a deployability-focused paper this is the single most-asked-for table by anyone considering using your workflow, and it remains absent. Moving it from claim into Table form is cheap and would meaningfully change the credibility of ``minutes per configuration.''
2. **MoE Skewed Zipf exponent / routing-histogram calibration is still undisclosed.** Iter-13 W1, Q1, and the Limitation (i) sketch are unchanged. ``Skewed top-2 dispatch'' appears once in Table~4 (line 289) and is never quantified --- no Zipf $s$, no reference histogram from Mixtral / DeepSeek-MoE / GShard / Switch Transformer, no link to a published expert-routing measurement. Because three MoE cells alone drive the headline $-11.8\%$ FBfly margin, the headline is unfalsifiable until a deployer can check whether their MoE skew matches yours. This is the highest-leverage disclosure that is *still* missing in iter-14, and it does not require new experiments --- it requires one sentence of the form ``our MoE Skewed sets Zipf$(s{=}\alpha)$ over $E$ experts with top-2 dispatch, which approximates the routing-load distribution reported in [cite].'' I would readily flip my score on this single change.
3. **No real-LLM-trace cross-validation, no FPGA / hardware co-validation, no end-to-end wall-clock translation, NL\% under workload mixing.** All four were W1 / W3 / Q1 / Q5 in iter-13 and remain explicitly noted as future work in Limitations (i)/(iii). I accept the position that these are research-program-scale follow-ups and not realistically blocker-class for a single conference submission, but I want to record honestly that none of them moved between iter-13 and iter-14, and that the ``deployability'' framing in the abstract and conclusion is therefore still stronger than the simulator-only evidence supports. The honest move would be to soften the framing slightly --- e.g., ``a deployable rule of thumb under cycle-accurate simulation'' rather than ``a deployable rule of thumb'' bare --- but this is a presentation nit, not a technical objection.
4. **The pooled $\rho{=}0.83$ Spearman is still computed across pooled $K\!\in\!\{16,32\}$ and $N\!\in\!\{4,8\}$ cells.** I noted this as a Detailed Comment on Section~4 in iter-13 (line 213--214 in the iter-13 file): part of what looks like an NL\%-driven correlation is the $K{=}32$-has-more-headroom trend. Iter-14 has not added the within-$(K,N)$-strata $\rho$ values I requested. The Spearman number is therefore still the optimistic version. This is minor relative to W1--W2 above.

## Questions for Authors

(Most iter-13 questions remain open; I prioritize the ones most cheaply addressable.)

1. **MoE skew calibration (highest priority, single-sentence change).** What Zipf exponent $s$ over how many experts $E$ does your MoE Skewed top-2 dispatch use? Which published MoE measurement (Mixtral 8x7B routing histogram, DeepSeek-MoE, GShard, Switch Transformer) is it calibrated to, even loosely? The whole headline number rides on this.
2. **Wall-clock breakdown.** A single Table with rows ``BookSim training-sample generation (1408 samples / 28 cells = X cells * Y BookSim runs each at Z s = T1 hours, amortized)'' / ``MLP training (T2)'' / ``one cell's 16-seed * 1000-ep REINFORCE + 48 BookSim candidates (T3)''. With T1 amortized correctly, ``minutes per configuration'' becomes either credible or it does not.
3. **Within-$(K,N)$-strata Spearman.** Compute the four $\rho$ values for the $\{(K,N)\}$ panels separately, and report whether the pooled $\rho{=}0.83$ is dominated by within-strata workload-NL\% rank or by across-strata $K$-rank. One small extra row in Section~4 or Table~4.
4. **Adversarial-traffic deadlock check.** As I asked in iter-13 Q7, can you confirm that BookSim's deadlock detector does not fire on any of the 28 RL-WS allocations under saturating injection? The $\le$48 BookSim runs per cell already exist; running each one further into saturation is a wall-clock add, not a methodological add.
5. **Multi-run RL variance.** Iter-13 Q4 unchanged: at minimum, on the three MoE Skewed cells driving the headline, please report mean$\pm$std of final BookSim latency across $\ge$3 independent end-to-end RL re-runs (different RNG for the 16 seeds). Limitation (v) acknowledges this is missing; the cost on three cells is small.

## Detailed Comments

**Abstract (line 31).** The MoE-driven dominance of the FBfly aggregate gap is not yet flagged in the abstract. iter-13 showed the same pattern; iter-14 added the disclosure to the Limitations and Conclusion but the abstract reader still sees the unconditional ``$-83.2\%$ peak / 24/28 wins / 35.6\% mean'' framing. One half-sentence would suffice: ``the aggregate FBfly gap is dominated by MoE Skewed; excluding MoE, the per-cell mean reduction is $-3.2\%$ rather than $-11.8\%$.'' This is a presentation nit but it is the same nit I raised in iter-13 and it is still cheap.

**Section~4, line 213--215.** The NL\%-as-classifier framing now leans on three numbers: pooled $\rho{=}0.83$, $-19.0\%$ mean reduction over FBfly on the 16 high-NL cells, and ``RL-WS matches FBfly within simulation noise'' on the 12 low-NL cells. The first is fine but unsplit by $(K,N)$ (W4 above); the second is not in any table I can find by that exact number --- Table~6 has per-workload $-63.1/-4.2/-3.9/-4.8\%$ for the four high-NL workload aggregates; the $-19.0\%$ figure in line 215 is the unweighted mean across these four, but a reader cannot trace it without doing the arithmetic. Adding ``(unweighted mean of the four high-NL workload entries in Table~6)'' or putting the stratum mean directly into Table~6's totals row would close that small loop.

**Section~5.2, line 257.** This paragraph is now my favorite paragraph in the paper. It draws the right boundary between surrogate quality and end-to-end safety: the surrogate is a within-cell ranker, the BookSim fallback is the safety mechanism. Excellent.

**Section~6, line 447.** Same prose as iter-13: ``RL-WS takes minutes per configuration (1000 episodes $\times$ 16 warm-start seeds plus BookSim selection over $\le$48 candidates)''. No table. The iter-14 changelog claims a §VI.C cost breakdown was added but I find no such subsection or table; the only ablation table is Table~7 (BookSim-best source decomposition + fallback activation), which is design-choice attribution rather than wall-clock cost. Either the table exists in a draft I am not seeing, or the changelog overstates this revision. Please add it or correct the changelog.

**Limitation (iv), line 487.** Rate-aware surrogate scoping is precisely correct; the new framing makes the safety-vs-generalization distinction unambiguous. This should resolve referee concerns about ``trained on the cells it grades.''

**Limitation (ii), line 487.** MoE-dependence disclosure in Limitations + Conclusion is the right move. Combined with Section~5.3, no honest reviewer can claim the paper hides this. I note this as a strength.

## Comparison to iter-13

**What moved up.** Surrogate scoping (W1 in iter-13 $\to$ resolved); multi-warm-start decomposition (Q3 partially answered: 17/11 split is in Table~7); MoE-dependence disclosure now in Limitations + Conclusion (was only in 5.3 in iter-13); numerical consistency on $\rho$ and on Greedy/FBfly/RL means (was a small drift in iter-13).

**What did not move.** Real-LLM-trace cross-validation (W1); MoE Zipf calibration (W2 / Q1); end-to-end wall-clock table (Q3); NL\% under workload mixing (Q5); within-$(K,N)$-strata Spearman (DC on Section~4); deadlock proof (Q7); CPElide / CATCH / Actuary uncited.

**Net effect on my score.** The disclosures-and-scoping changes are real and they do remove the iter-13 weakness W1 (``surrogate trained on the very cells it is graded on''). They do not address the iter-13 weakness W2 (``MoE skew is unanchored'') --- which is the single highest-leverage open item, and the cheapest to fix. They partially address weakness W3 (``end-to-end deploy-time cost is undisclosed'') in the warm-start-source decomposition but leave the wall-clock table I asked for unfilled despite the iter-14 changelog claiming a §VI.C cost breakdown. So the calibration is: iter-14 is a real improvement on iter-13 along the surrogate-scoping and disclosure axes, but the central deployability gap (no real trace, no wall-clock, no skew calibration) is essentially unchanged.

I move from **3.0 (Borderline / lean Weak Reject)** in iter-13 to **3.5 (Borderline / lean Weak Accept)** in iter-14. Honestly, that is the move that disclosure-only changes earn: I trust the paper more, but the deployability claim still rides on simulator-only synthetic-traffic evidence with an uncalibrated MoE skew. The Zipf calibration sentence (Q1 above) would push me to a clean 4.0 / Accept; absent it, I remain on the accept side of the borderline by a small margin.

## Rating

- Novelty: 3 / 5 (unchanged; NL\%-as-classifier + dual warm-start + rate-aware surrogate is a sensible composition, each piece incremental, the workflow framing is the novelty.)
- Technical Quality: 3.5 / 5 (up from 3.0 in iter-13; surrogate is now correctly scoped as in-distribution and the BookSim fallback is the named safety mechanism, but no multi-run variance, no real-LLM-trace, no formal deadlock proof.)
- Significance: 3 / 5 (unchanged; deployment-classifier framing is useful, but the $-83.2\%$ headline still rests on three MoE cells whose skew calibration is unspecified.)
- Presentation: 4 / 5 (unchanged; numerical consistency restored, MoE dependence promoted to Limitations and Conclusion, but the abstract still leads with the unstratified headline numbers.)
- Overall: 3.5 / 5 (up from 3.0 in iter-13.)
- Confidence: 4 / 5 (unchanged.)

## Decision

**Borderline (leaning Weak Accept).** Iter-14's surrogate-scoping reframing (W1 from iter-13 resolved) and the multi-warm-start decomposition in Table~7 are real improvements that materially change the technical-quality picture. The MoE-dependence disclosure in Limitations + Conclusion is honest enough that I no longer worry the paper is concealing the result distribution. I would move to a clean Weak Accept (4.0) if the authors (a) add one sentence calibrating MoE Skewed to a published expert-routing histogram (Mixtral / DeepSeek-MoE / GShard / Switch Transformer) with an explicit Zipf exponent, and (b) deliver the wall-clock cost-breakdown table that the iter-14 changelog claims was added but that I cannot find in the paper. Both are small, both are cheap, both are the same items I asked for in iter-13.
