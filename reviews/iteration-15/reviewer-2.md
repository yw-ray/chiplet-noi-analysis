## Summary

Iter-15 is a targeted-disclosure revision of iter-14, narrowly scoped to the two items I called out as conditions for moving from 3.5 (Borderline / lean Weak Accept) to a clean 4.0 (Weak Accept). Both are now in the paper. (a) MoE Skewed is calibrated: Section~6.1 line 280 now states ``Zipf$(s{=}1.5)$ over $K{-}1$ candidate experts with top-2 dispatch, averaged over $n_\text{seeds}{=}5$ random samples,'' explicitly anchored to DeepSeek-V3~\cite{deepseekv3} and Mixtral-8x7B~\cite{mixtral} as the production-MoE comparators and to GShard~\cite{gshard} as the uniform-baseline contrast that older NoI studies implicitly used. (b) Wall-clock cost is now broken down: a new Section~6.4 ``Wall-Clock Cost'' (line 406) and Table~9 (`tab:cost`) report surrogate-v2 training as $\sim$3 minutes total amortized over all 28 cells, RL training as 3--5 minutes per cell parallel across 16 seeds, BookSim final selection as 10--40 minutes per cell ($\le$48 RL candidates plus 3 baselines at 4 rates each), and a per-cell parallel total of 15--45 minutes, with the full 28-cell sweep at 10--20 hours. The headline numbers (35.6\% mean saving, 24/28 strict FBfly wins, $-83.2\%$ peak on MoE Skewed $K{=}32, N{=}8, b{=}4\times$, $\rho{=}0.83$, $-11.8\%$ vs $-3.2\%$ MoE-excluded gap) are unchanged from iter-14.

## Strengths

1. **MoE skew is now anchored to production measurements.** Section~6.1 line 280 spells out the parameterization (Zipf$(s{=}1.5)$, top-2, $K{-}1$ candidate experts, 5 seeds averaged) and the production analogues (DeepSeek-V3, Mixtral-8x7B), and it goes one constructive step further by naming GShard's top-$k$-uniform assumption as the implicit straw-man that older NoI MoE studies used. The sentence ``Zipf$(1.5)$ places approximately 36\% of total dispatch on the top-2 ranks at $K{=}32$, the resulting traffic matrix is both highly non-local (NL\%${=}91$) and strongly skewed'' is exactly the bridge between the parameterization and the empirical phenomenon (FBfly under-provisions heavy expert pairs by construction) that drives the $-83.2\%$ headline. A reader can now check ``does my deployment look like Zipf$(1.5)$ top-2?'' rather than ``does my deployment look like the authors' undisclosed skew?'' This was my single highest-leverage open ask in iter-13 and iter-14, and it is now closed.
2. **Wall-clock cost-breakdown table now exists as a real table, not a sentence.** Table~9 separates the four cost phases I asked for: amortized one-time (surrogate training over 1408 samples, $\sim$3 min total), per-cell RL training ($\sim$3--5 min for 16 seeds × 1000 episodes parallel), per-cell BookSim selection ($\sim$10--40 min for $\le$48 RL candidates × 4 rates + 3 baseline configs × 4 rates), deterministic baselines ($<$1 s each), per-cell total parallelized ($\sim$15--45 min), and full 28-cell sweep ($\sim$10--20 hr). The amortization of surrogate training over the 28 cells is explicit ($\sim$3 min total, not per-cell), which was the specific arithmetic point I flagged in iter-14 W1. The follow-up paragraph at line 441 also correctly reframes the cost as a design-time-once exercise and points to the NL\% deployment classifier as the way to skip RL-WS entirely on low-NL cells. ``Minutes per configuration'' is now a quantified claim rather than handwaving.
3. **The two iter-14 fixes are precisely scoped, not gold-plated.** The iter-15 changes are narrow: one paragraph in Section~6.1 (MoE calibration), one new subsection 6.4 + Table~9 (wall-clock), citation additions (`mixtral`, `deepseekv3`, `gshard`). No ablation tables were rearranged, no headline numbers moved, no figures were redrawn. This is the right kind of revision between iterations: you asked for two things and you got two things, no scope creep. I appreciate this discipline.
4. **Honest in-distribution scoping of the surrogate (carryover from iter-14).** Section~5.2 line 257 retains the iter-14 reframing that the surrogate is a within-cell ranker and the BookSim fallback is the safety mechanism. Limitation (iv) at line 526 keeps this distinction intact. This was iter-13 weakness W1 and remains resolved.
5. **Decomposed multi-warm-start contribution (carryover from iter-14).** Table~7 still reports BookSim-best source = 17/28 greedy-warm + 11/28 FBfly-warm and the 22/2/4 split of raw RL vs $\min(\text{greedy}, \text{FBfly})$, which together establish that both warm-start sources individually pull weight. Iter-15 does not regress on this.

Limitations (i) and (vii)/(viii) at lines 526 honestly acknowledge it. I accept that these are research-program-scale follow-ups and not realistically blocker-class for a single conference submission, and the abstract/conclusion framing is now appropriately ``deployable rule of thumb under cycle-accurate simulation'' in spirit if not in literal phrasing. Calling this out as a standing limitation rather than a blocker: my score does not move on this.
2. **Multi-run RL variance still missing on the three MoE Skewed cells driving the headline.** I asked for this as far back as iter-13 Q4 and iter-14 Q5: at minimum, on MoE Skewed $K{=}32, N{=}4$, $K{=}32, N{=}8$, and $K{=}16, N{=}8$ at $b{=}4\times$, please report mean$\pm$std of final BookSim latency across $\ge$3 independent end-to-end RL re-runs (different RNG for the 16 warm-start seeds and the 1000 REINFORCE episodes). Limitation (v) at line 526 explicitly acknowledges this is missing. The cost is small (3 cells × 3 reruns × $\sim$30 min selection $\approx$ 4--5 hours) and it directly attacks the obvious referee question ``is the $-83.2\%$ peak reproducible or is it the lucky tail of a single seed sweep?'' Iter-15 does not address this and I would not insist on it for a 4.0 score, but I want to record it as a standing weakness for any iter-16 revision.
3. **Within-$(K,N)$-strata Spearman not added.** Same as iter-14 W4. Section~4 line 215 still reports the pooled $\rho{=}0.83$. The iter-15 additions are scoped to MoE calibration and wall-clock cost, so I am not surprised this did not move; it is a minor cleanup that would tighten Section~4 without changing the story.

## Questions for Authors

1. **MoE skew calibration follow-up.** Now that Zipf$(s{=}1.5)$ is the published parameterization: have you confirmed that the dispatch-share-on-top-2 figure (you say $\sim$36\% at $K{=}32$) lies within the empirical envelope of the cited DeepSeek-V3 and Mixtral routing histograms? A single sentence to that effect (``Zipf$(1.5)$ at $K{=}32$ places $\sim$36\% of dispatch on the top-2 ranks, within the $\sim$30--45\% range reported across DeepSeek-V3 layers and Mixtral-8x7B layers'') would strengthen the calibration further but is not blocking.
2. **Wall-clock cost --- BookSim per-run time.** Table~9 reports BookSim selection at 10--40 minutes per cell for $\le$48 RL candidates × 4 rates + 3 baselines × 4 rates = $\le$204 BookSim runs. That implies a per-BookSim-run wall-clock of roughly 3--12 seconds. This is plausible for a small $K{=}16, N{=}4$ cell but seems optimistic for $K{=}32, N{=}8$. Could you clarify whether the 10--40 minute range is dominated by the small cells (and the large cells are toward the upper bound or above) or whether per-run time was held roughly constant? A footnote or a per-cell-size breakdown would close this loop.
3. **Multi-run RL variance.** As above (W2). Three MoE cells, three reruns each, mean$\pm$std --- the cheapest possible variance check. I am not insisting for 4.0 but I am asking for iter-16.
4. **Adversarial-traffic deadlock check.** Open since iter-13 Q7 and iter-14 Q4. Section~7 line 491 (``Deadlock freedom'') gives the construction argument (XY-style dimension-ordered routing with each express link assigned to the same VC class as the dimension whose hop count it reduces, two VC subsets per dimension, ordered channel-dependency graph remains acyclic). This is a sufficient sketch for a workshop / borderline conference submission and I now consider Q4 closed; a Duato-style formal proof remains future work but the construction is convincing enough.

## Decision

**Weak Accept (4.0).** Iter-15 delivers exactly the two items I conditioned the move from 3.5 to 4.0 on: MoE Skewed is calibrated to Zipf$(s{=}1.5)$ top-2 with production MoE citations (DeepSeek-V3, Mixtral, GShard), and the wall-clock cost is broken down into a real table with amortized surrogate training, per-cell RL, per-cell BookSim selection, and total parallelized cost. The previously honest scoping of the surrogate (in-distribution ranker + BookSim fallback as safety) and the multi-warm-start decomposition (17 greedy-warm / 11 FBfly-warm BookSim-best source) carry over from iter-14 unchanged. The paper now passes my deployability bar: an architect reading it can answer (i) does my workload's NL\% and skew look like the high-payoff regime, (ii) what does it cost me to run this workflow once at design time, and (iii) what is the worst case if RL-WS misfires. Standing weaknesses --- no real LLM trace, no hardware validation, no multi-run RL variance --- are honestly disclosed as limitations rather than concealed, and they are research-program-scale follow-ups rather than blocker-class for a single conference submission. I would still encourage the authors to add the multi-run RL variance check on the three MoE Skewed cells driving the headline (Q3) and to clean up the three orphaned bibliography entries before camera-ready, but neither is blocking the accept decision.
