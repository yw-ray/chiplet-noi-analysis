# Review — Reviewer 1 (Architecture Expert), Iteration 15

## Summary
Iteration 15 makes two of the three iter-14 deltas I asked for. The PARL contrast is materially strengthened: §II Related Work now does a paragraph-length three-axis design-space differentiation (workload predictor / warm-start initialization / safety guarantee) and ties each axis to a specific numerical artifact already in the paper (Eq.~(6) fallback activates 4/28 cells; the 17 greedy-warm + 11 FBfly-warm split in Table~7; the NL$\le$50\% deployment-classifier bypass). The MoE Skewed workload is now disclosed with a Zipf exponent of $s{=}1.5$, top-2 dispatch, $n_\text{seeds}{=}5$ averaging, and explicit citations to DeepSeek-V3 and Mixtral as the empirical anchor for the skew. A new §VI.D ("Wall-Clock Cost") provides a per-cell breakdown of design-time cost (15--45 minutes per cell, 10--20 hours for the full 28-cell sweep) that I did not ask for in iter-14 but that I find genuinely informative for an architecture reader trying to budget the method against a one-shot silicon-lifetime allocation. The third iter-14 ask --- per-method wire-mm$^2$ Pareto curve in (cost, latency) space, which is the substantive remaining gap from iter-13 --- is **not** addressed; Table~9 (now Table~10 in iter-15? still labeled `tab:physical` --- §VI.G "Physical Overhead") is unchanged, and §VI.G still asserts that RL-WS "inherits the same per-link overhead as greedy" without showing the per-method distance distribution that would actually make that claim falsifiable. I therefore raise the score by a quarter-point, not the full half I would assign for closing both gaps.

## Strengths

1. **[S1, retained] The structural argument is unchanged and remains the strongest part of the paper.** Section~III ($\Theta(K^{3/2})$ phantom load, Theorem~1, Corollary~1) and the routing-independence study in Table~3 are both untouched and continue to do something most chiplet-NoI papers miss: they decouple the *topological* tax of multi-hop adjacency from any specific routing policy. The 4×4 worked example ($\alpha = 16\times$, line 151) makes the bound easy to sanity-check.

2. **[S2, retained] NL% as a single static, simulation-free predictor at the right level of abstraction.** The pre-routing-demand-matrix definition (Eq. lines 207--210), the layout-dependence honesty in §IV (line 212: "$\mathrm{NL\%}$ depends on \textit{both} the demand matrix $T$ and the chiplet-to-grid mapping $G$"), and the $\rho{=}0.83$ pooled correlation across 28 cells are still the cleanest design-time-knob story I have seen in this manuscript family.

3. **[S3, retained] Multi-warm-start with Eq.~(6) as a labeled measured-latency safety guarantee.** Eq.~(6) is unchanged, the 4/28 fallback activation rate is unchanged, and the 17 greedy-warm vs 11 FBfly-warm BookSim-best split in Table~7 is unchanged. These are still load-bearing; iter-15 does not regress on any of them.

4. **[S4, strengthened in iter-15] The PARL contrast is now a substantive three-axis differentiation tied to the paper's own numerical evidence.** The iter-14 limitations entry "PARL targets a complementary problem...we position it qualitatively instead of attempting a head-to-head reproduction" is now backed by an explicit §II paragraph (lines 71--72 in my read) that breaks the design-space difference into (i) workload predictor (PARL has none; we have NL%-based deployment classifier with empirical bypass criterion at NL$\le$50\%), (ii) initialization (PARL is cold-start maskable PPO; we are multi-warm-start with both heuristics individually load-bearing per Table~7), and (iii) safety (PARL's RL output is the final allocation; we measure-then-select via Eq.~(6) with empirical 4/28 activation). Each axis is connected to a specific number that is already in our experiments. This is the closest a *non-reproduction* contrast can get to substantive: instead of "PARL is different in three ways", the paper now says "PARL is different along three axes, here is the paper's own evidence that each axis is independently load-bearing." I credit this as a real strengthening even though it is still not a numerical head-to-head.

5. **[S5, new in iter-15] Wall-clock cost is now disclosed.** §VI.D (Table~8) reports a per-cell budget breakdown: ~3 min one-time surrogate training, 3--5 min RL training (16 seeds parallel), 10--40 min BookSim selection (the dominant cost), and a $\sim$15--45-min per-cell total parallelized on 8 cores, summing to $\sim$10--20 hours for the full 28-cell sweep. This was not on my iter-14 request list but I find it genuinely informative for an architecture reader: it makes the "design-time exercise that produces a one-shot allocation deployed for the silicon lifetime" framing in line 441 falsifiable, lets a hardware planner see exactly what they are buying, and makes the "fall back to FBfly on NL$\le$50\%" recommendation in §VII concretely cost-justified. I would have liked the table to also list the *single-thread* total (so a reader can sanity-check the parallelization assumption) but the parallel total is the architecturally relevant number.

6. **[S6, strengthened in iter-15] MoE Zipf calibration is now empirically anchored.** §VI.A line 280 specifies $s{=}1.5$, top-2 dispatch, $n_\text{seeds}{=}5$ averaging, with citations to DeepSeek-V3~\cite{deepseekv3} and Mixtral~\cite{mixtral} for production-MoE concentration ratios, and explicitly contrasts against uniform top-$k$ dispatch as in GShard~\cite{gshard}. This was a soft concern in iter-14 (the headline $-83.2\%$ MoE result rests on this calibration choice and iter-14 did not document it). The iter-15 version makes the calibration choice both reproducible and defended against the obvious "you cherry-picked a skew that favors workload-aware placement" pushback: the cited concentration ratios are themselves empirical observations on production models.

## Weaknesses

1. **[W1, retained from iter-14, retained from iter-13] The express-link physical model is still too thin for an architecture venue.** Table~10 ("Physical Overhead of Express Links") is unchanged. It still lists wire length / area / power / latency for $d{=}1\!\dots\!4$ as "CoWoS-class estimates" without a derivation, a citation to a specific wire-delay model (Bakoglu, ITRS, or a recent CoWoS-S/L characterization paper), driver/repeater assumption, or via penalty. The maximum express distance $D$ is still never reported per cell (line 223 still says "$d \in [2, D]$" without specifying $D$ for $K{=}32$ grids where Manhattan distance can reach $\sqrt{32}+\sqrt{32}{-}2 \approx 9$). For DAC/DATE this remains the single most important remaining fix. Iter-15 has had room to address this --- §VI.D Wall-Clock Cost is a brand-new section, and addressing physical modeling instead would have shifted weight toward the *architecture-track* concerns rather than the *engineering-cost* concerns. I document this trade-off explicitly so the authors can decide in iter-16.

2. **[W2, partially closed in iter-15; still not a numerical head-to-head] PARL is now contrasted along three axes but still has no numerical bar in Table~6.** The new §II paragraph is the most credible the paper has been on this front, but the contrast is still *evidence about our own design's components*, not *side-by-side measurement of PARL's allocations on the 28-cell benchmark*. The architecture-track question is not "does the paper articulate three design-space differences?" but "on the same 28 cells, how does PARL's *placement decision* (which non-adjacent pairs receive express links under a given budget) compare to ours when both are evaluated in BookSim?" That question is still answerable in principle: the surrogate v2 + BookSim pipeline already evaluates 50 candidates per cell (48 RL + 2 baselines), and adding a third baseline that follows PARL's interference-score ranking would cost the same wall-clock as adding a third deterministic baseline. The cost-benefit calculus has shifted: the iter-15 design-space contrast is good enough that, for a borderline-accept paper, the marginal value of a numerical PARL bar is reduced --- but it is not zero. I would still expect either (a) a numerical PARL bar or (b) an explicit "we ported PARL's reward function and on the 4 cells we tested, it produces allocations within $\pm X\%$ of greedy" sub-experiment in any iter-16 revision aimed at the architecture track. For a workshop or DATE poster the iter-15 framing is sufficient.

3. **[W3, retained from iter-14, retained from iter-13] No per-method wire-mm$^2$ Pareto curve.** §VI.G still frames RL-WS as "inheriting the same per-link overhead as greedy" because both consume the same total link budget. This is *only* true if the *distance distribution* of the chosen express links is the same; it is not, by construction --- MoE Skewed reallocates capacity onto a few heavy long-distance pairs while FBfly spreads links over $d{=}2$ row/column shortcuts. The iter-15 paper still does not report (a) the per-method distribution of express-link distances, (b) the total wire-mm$^2$ per method (which would convert Table~10's per-link wire-area into a per-method total), or (c) the resulting (wire-mm$^2$, latency) Pareto plot. Without this, the headline $-83.2\%$ on MoE Skewed cannot be converted into a fair cost-per-performance ratio: a reader does not know whether RL-WS achieves the saving by using the same wire area more cleverly or by opportunistically using more wire on a few long links. This was my second iter-14 ask and it remains the substantive remaining gap. The MoE-honesty disclosure (excluding MoE, $-3.2\%$) and the deployment-classifier framing only partially mitigate the concern: they bound the *headline* gap but not the *physical-cost* picture.

4. **[W4, partially closed; minor residue, retained from iter-14] The $-5.3\%$ low-NL margin claim is still not directly readable from Table~6.** The iter-15 abstract and §VI.B still say low-NL cells "still strictly beat FBfly on 8/12 with max $-5.3\%$ margin." Table~6 reports per-workload means of $-0.8\%, -3.1\%, -2.4\%$ on Tree, Ring, Pipeline. The $-5.3\%$ is presumably a per-cell maximum within those 8 wins, but it is not exposed in any table. A one-line footnote in Table~6 listing the per-cell max (per workload or pooled) would close this; the wall-clock table got added in iter-15, so adding a footnote here is feasible.

## Questions for Authors

1. **[Q1, retained from iter-14, retained from iter-13]** What is the maximum express distance $D$ in each of the 28 cells, and how is it set? Is it a fixed constant, a function of $\sqrt{K}$, or a per-cell beachfront/PHY budget? Please report (a) $D$ used per cell, (b) the distribution over $d$ of express links chosen by greedy, FBfly, and RL-WS, and (c) the resulting total wire-mm$^2$ per method. Without this, Table~10 (physical overhead) still cannot be tied back to the placements actually used in Table~6.

2. **[Q2, retained from iter-14]** The deadlock-freedom argument on lines 491--492 still says express links inherit the VC class of the dimension whose hop count they reduce, with the explicit two-VC-subset construction now stated in iter-15 ("a packet only consumes $X$-VCs before $Y$-VCs"). What about express links at distance $d{\ge}2$ that move along *both* X and Y (e.g., a diagonal $d{=}3$ link that bypasses 2 X-hops and 1 Y-hop)? Does the placer restrict the express candidate set to axis-aligned pairs only, or does it allow diagonals --- and if diagonals are allowed, how does the express link inherit "the dimension whose hop count it reduces" when it reduces *both*? A one-line clarification in the iter-15 deadlock paragraph would resolve this.

3. **[Q3, new in iter-15]** §VI.D (Wall-Clock Cost) reports the BookSim selection step as 10--40 min per cell, dominating the total. The wall-clock total scales with the 48-RL-candidate × 4-rate evaluation contract. If a future deployment uses $k{=}1$ instead of top-3 candidate retention, the BookSim selection drops to 16-RL-candidate × 4-rate $\approx$ 1/3 of current cost. Do you have data on how the BookSim-best-cell margin (the $-3.2\%$ ex-MoE per-cell mean reduction) degrades when $k$ is reduced from 3 to 1? This would let a deployer trade off design-time cost against allocation quality in a principled way. A single sentence answer in §VI.D would suffice.

4. **[Q4, retained from iter-14]** Table~7 reports 17 greedy-warm + 11 FBfly-warm BookSim-best selections, and §II now says "both warm-start sources are individually necessary in practice." Does the 17:11 split correlate with NL%, MoE-vs-non-MoE, or budget multiplier? If yes, that is a stronger predictor story (a one-line addition to §VI.C); if no, the multi-warm-start is doing exploration work that NL% alone does not predict.

5. **[Q5, new in iter-15]** §II says "the iter-12 ablation that informed our design" in passing --- this is internal phrasing that should not survive into the camera-ready draft. Please remove the iter-N reference in the final manuscript.

## Detailed Comments

**§II, PARL paragraph (lines 70--72).** This is the single largest iter-15 addition relative to iter-14. The three-axis design-space contrast is now specific and tied to the paper's own evidence: (i) NL%-bypass at NL$\le$50\% (specific bypass criterion), (ii) 17 greedy-warm + 11 FBfly-warm split (specific evidence both warm-starts are necessary), (iii) Eq.~(6) post-hoc fallback activates 4/28 (specific evidence safety mechanism is load-bearing). ## Calibration: does the strengthened PARL contrast satisfy the architecture-track concern?

**Short answer:** it raises the score by a quarter of a point but not a full half, because the contrast is now *substantive* (three axes, each tied to specific paper evidence) rather than *cosmetic* (iter-14's "qualitative positioning") --- but it is still *evidence about our own design's components*, not *side-by-side measurement of PARL on our 28 cells*.

**Long answer:** for a workshop or DATE poster track, the iter-15 PARL contrast is now sufficient: a reader who reads §II and Table~7 ablation can connect each design-space differentiation to a specific number that is independently load-bearing, and the limitations list (vi) honestly flags the missing reproduction. For the full DAC/DATE architecture track, the bar is "the central claim 'RL-WS strictly beats the strongest learned baseline' is empirically supported"; iter-15 supports the weaker claim "RL-WS strictly beats the strongest *heuristic* baseline (FBfly) and is differentiated from the strongest *learned* baseline (PARL) along three independently load-bearing axes." That weaker claim is now defensible. Whether it clears the architecture-track bar depends on whether the reviewer interprets "strongest baseline" as "strongest deployed-and-comparable baseline" (in which case FBfly suffices for a placement-only paper) or "strongest published baseline" (in which case PARL still needs a numerical bar). I lean toward the latter interpretation for DAC/DATE, which is why I am moving to "Weak Accept" rather than "Accept."

## Decision
**Weak Accept** (was Borderline / lean Weak Accept in iter-14). The iter-15 changes substantively close two of the three iter-14 deltas: PARL is now contrasted along three design-space axes tied to the paper's own numerical evidence (no longer a cosmetic deferral), and the MoE Zipf calibration is now reproducible and empirically anchored to DeepSeek-V3 and Mixtral. The wall-clock cost table is a genuinely useful addition I did not request. The remaining iter-14 gap --- per-method wire-mm$^2$ Pareto curve and per-cell maximum express distance $D$ --- is unchanged from iter-14 and remains the single largest residual concern for a DAC/DATE architecture-track submission. I am moving to "Weak Accept" because (a) the trajectory iter-13 → iter-14 → iter-15 is unambiguously upward at every iteration, (b) the iter-15 changes are exactly the right ones for closing the iter-14 deltas they address, and (c) the strongest remaining concern (physical-cost accounting) is venue-dependent rather than fundamental to the central claim. For a workshop or DATE poster track, this is now publishable as-is. For the full DAC/DATE architecture track, I would still want either the wire-mm$^2$ Pareto or a numerical PARL bar in iter-16 before recommending strong accept; but neither is required for a Weak Accept at this iteration.
