# Reviewer 3 (ML / LLM Application Expert) — Iteration 15

**Paper:** Predict, Place, Refine: Non-Locality-Guided Express Link Placement for LLM Chiplet Networks

## Summary of Iter-15 Changes Relative to My Iter-14 Review

The authors made three targeted edits between iter-14 and iter-15:

- **§VI Setup MoE calibration paragraph (line 280).** A new paragraph adds production-trace anchoring for the Zipf$(s{=}1.5)$ MoE workload: DeepSeek-V3 and Mixtral-8x7B are cited explicitly as MoE language models whose measured expert-popularity skew sits in the same regime, and GShard's uniform top-$k$ baseline is named explicitly as the foil that earlier MoE NoI studies used (and that conflates load balancing with placement). The bibliography now adds `deepseekv3`, `mixtral`, and `gshard` entries (lines 581–588).
- **§VI.D Wall-Clock Cost subsection (lines 406–441) + Table 9.** A new subsection itemizes per-cell wall-clock in four phases (one-time surrogate training $\sim$3 min total; per-cell RL training 3–5 min; per-cell BookSim selection 10–40 min; deterministic baselines $<$1 s), totaling 15–45 min per cell parallelized and 10–20 hr for the full 28-cell sweep.
- **§II Related Work PARL contrast strengthened (line 71).** The PARL paragraph is now a numbered three-axis contrast (predictor / warm-start / safety), each axis supported by numerical evidence from this paper's results: NL\%-classifier closes a per-workload-RL-cost gap that PARL leaves open; the 17/11 greedy-vs-FBfly warm-start split argues both seeds are necessary; the $4/28$ post-hoc BookSim fallback rate is named as the explicit safety-guarantee mechanism PARL lacks.

## Are W3, W4, W5 Genuinely Closed?

**W3 (MoE software baseline). Partially closed at the citation level, not at the experimental level.**

The new line 280 paragraph does what the prompt characterizes: it cites DeepSeek-V3 and Mixtral-8x7B as production references with comparable Zipf concentration ratios, and explicitly contrasts the Zipf-skewed dispatcher assumption against GShard's uniform top-$k$. This is a meaningful citation-level fix and addresses my W6 reference gap from iter-14 in passing. But the underlying critique I raised — *what happens to the $-83\%$ MoE headline if you fix Zipf skew at the dispatcher level via Tutel, DeepSpeed-MoE, or DeepSeek-V3-style expert affinity placement?* — is not engaged. There is no paragraph in §V.C (per-cell analysis) saying "if expert co-location reduces concentration ratio, NL\% drops, and that is exactly what the deployment classifier is meant to flag." That is the framing that would convert the MoE headline from an apples-to-oranges comparison (RL-WS in hardware vs. uniform-MoE software baseline) into a positioned claim ("RL-WS is the hardware solution; software-level expert co-location is the dispatcher solution; both are valid and complementary; NL\% tells you whether you need the hardware fix"). The prompt itself flags this gap correctly: production traces are now cited but no software baseline runs. **From "blocking ceiling on significance" to "the citation gap is closed; the experimental gap and the framing gap remain open."**

The framing gap is the cheaper of the two and would have moved my score. A single sentence in §V.C — "Tutel/DeepSpeed-MoE attack Zipf skew at the dispatcher level by co-locating hot experts; this lowers NL\% and therefore moves a workload across our deployment-classifier boundary, making RL-WS unnecessary on that workload — which is the prediction NL\% is designed to make" — would make the position defensible without running anything. As written, the LLM-systems reader still has to construct that positioning themselves.

**W4 (wall-clock translation). Not closed. The new §VI.D table answers a different question.**

I want to be careful here because the new Table 9 is a genuinely useful addition and I do not want to penalize it for not being something it never claimed to be. The new table answers *design-time wall-clock cost of producing an allocation* (10–20 hr for the full sweep), which is its own legitimate concern and one I did not previously raise. What it does not answer — and what W4 specifically asked for — is *runtime wall-clock translation of the BookSim-cycle savings into an inference or training step*. Concretely: a Mixtral-8x7B forward pass spends some fraction $f$ of its time in expert-dispatch all-to-all collective transit, and reducing the BookSim cycles for that collective from 402.8 to 67.8 reduces step time by approximately $f \times (1 - 67.8/402.8) = 0.83 f$. Without an estimate of $f$ (even $f \in [0.05, 0.30]$ as a back-of-envelope range), the reader cannot answer "what fraction of a Mixtral training step does this paper's $-83\%$ headline save?" — and that is the question an ISCA Reviewer with LLM-systems instinct asks first.

The prompt is explicit that this distinction was understood: design-time cost was added, training-step Amdahl translation was not. I credit the design-time addition — it is a legitimate concern and it is well-presented — but it does not move the W4 needle.

**W5 (NCCL hierarchical AllReduce baseline). Not closed.**

No change here. Tree AR / Ring AR / All-to-all are still compared only against adjacent-uniform / greedy / FBfly hardware allocators, never against software-collective optimizations. NCCL-style topology-aware ring builders, 2D hierarchical AllReduce, and ZeRO++ are still uncited. This is the smaller of the three gaps because the paper's contribution is positioned as hardware-side allocation, but for an LLM-systems audience it is still the obvious "what about the software-level alternative?" question and an ISCA reviewer in the systems track will ask it.

## What Iter-15 Did Well

- **S1. PARL contrast is now load-bearing rather than rhetorical.** Iter-14's PARL paragraph stated the differences; iter-15 quantifies each axis with this paper's own numbers (NL\%-classifier, 17/11 warm-start split, 4/28 fallback rate). This is the right way to do related-work positioning, and an ISCA reviewer reading just §II will leave with a clear picture of why this paper is not subsumed by PARL.
- **S2. Production trace citations.** DeepSeek-V3 and Mixtral citations directly address my iter-14 W6 (then-trivial) gap. The GShard contrast is also useful — it names *the* prior assumption (uniform top-$k$) that this paper's choice of Zipf$(1.5)$ is differentiated against.
- **S3. Wall-clock cost Table 9.** Even though it does not address W4, it is well-itemized and credible (surrogate-once amortization, BookSim selection as the dominant cost, deterministic baselines as fast defaults, 15–45 min per cell). An architecture-track reviewer evaluating "is this method actually deployable at design time?" will find this table convincing. This is the kind of disclosure that stops an "is this PhD-thesis-only?" reject.

## Remaining Weaknesses (Iter-15)

- **W3-residual (framing).** No §V.C paragraph contrasting RL-WS-on-Zipf against Tutel/DeepSpeed-MoE expert co-location. Cheap to add (one paragraph, no new experiments), would close the positioning gap that the citation-only fix leaves open.
- **W3-deep (experimental).** No software-MoE baseline run. The "$-83\%$ on MoE" headline still sits against an FBfly hardware allocator only, not against a software dispatcher fix. This is the more expensive gap and I do not penalize iter-15 for not running it; I just do not credit closure.
- **W4 (Amdahl).** Single biggest unchanged gap. The cycle-level $-83\%$ has not been translated into any practitioner-facing wall-clock-of-step number. Even a one-paragraph back-of-envelope ("if expert-dispatch is $f \in [10\%, 25\%]$ of a Mixtral-8x7B forward pass, the headline corresponds to a step-time reduction of $8\%$–$21\%$") would move the LLM-systems-impact claim. Without this, the paper still cannot answer the practitioner's first question.
- **W5 (NCCL hierarchical).** Software AllReduce baselines still uncited and uncompared. Smaller gap because the paper is positioned as hardware allocation, but still a known LLM-systems blind spot.
- **N1 (carried from iter-14).** Abstract metric inconsistency unchanged: "$-19.0\%$ on high-NL cells" in §IV vs "$-63.1\%$ per-workload mean reduction relative to FBfly" for MoE in Table 7 — both correct under their respective averaging scopes, both still present in the abstract without explicit separation. A footnote would help.
- **N2 (carried). RL multi-seed variance study still future work** (Limitation v, line 526). The 4/28 minor-loss cells could fall on either side of zero under different RL retraining seeds, so the headline +35.6\% has no error bar. The post-hoc fallback bounds the worst case but not the per-cell variance of the headline.
- **N4 (new for iter-15). Wall-clock cost is design-time only.** The new §VI.D should ideally be paired with a single-paragraph deployment-time discussion: once the allocation is fixed, the runtime cost is zero (the allocation is committed to silicon) and only the design-time 10–20 hr matters. This is implicit in the surrounding text but the explicit statement would help — the framing "design-time minutes pays for runtime forever" is the right story to tell here and is currently latent.

## Detailed Comments (Iter-15 Specific)

- **L71 (PARL paragraph).** Strong rewrite. The numbered three-axis structure is concrete and each axis is anchored to a specific number from this paper's results table. Consider adding one more half-sentence to axis (i): "PARL's evaluation does not stratify wins by a workload statistic, so it cannot tell architects in advance which workloads are worth the RL cost — exactly the gap our NL\%-classifier closes."
- **L280 (MoE calibration paragraph).** The DeepSeek-V3 / Mixtral / GShard contrast is good, but the paragraph stops one sentence short of where it needs to. Add: "Software-level expert co-location (Tutel, DeepSpeed-MoE) reduces the effective Zipf concentration ratio at the dispatcher and is therefore a complementary mitigation; under the deployment-classifier framing, such co-location lowers NL\% and moves the workload across the threshold at which RL-WS becomes worth running. We treat hardware express placement and software dispatcher co-location as orthogonal axes and do not run a head-to-head comparison." This sentence costs nothing and addresses the W3-framing gap cleanly.
- **L411 (Table 9).** The "Total per-cell (parallelized, 8-core) 15–45 min" line is the load-bearing one, and the "Total for full 28-cell sweep 10–20 hr" line answers a question I did not raise but that an architecture reviewer will. Both are well-bounded ranges, not point estimates, which is the right level of precision. Strong addition.
- **L441 (post-Table-9 prose).** "The cost is acceptable for a design-time exercise that produces a one-shot allocation deployed in silicon for the lifetime of the package" is the correct framing. Consider strengthening: "Greedy and FBfly remain available as fast deterministic defaults (sub-second), and the NL\%-based deployment classifier lets architects skip RL-WS entirely on NL\%$\le$50\% workloads — turning the 10–20 hr full-sweep cost into a 5–10 hr cost in practice when low-NL cells are filtered out by the classifier." That converts the design-time table into a deployment-classifier multiplier.

## Questions for Authors (Carried + New)

- **Q1 (carried, W3-framing).** Could you add one paragraph in §V.C contrasting RL-WS-on-Zipf against Tutel/DeepSpeed-MoE expert co-location, even qualitatively? This would close the W3 framing gap without new experiments.
- **Q2 (carried, W4).** Could you add a back-of-envelope Amdahl translation: assuming all-to-all (or its workload-equivalent collective) is $f$ of a Mixtral-8x7B / DeepSeek-V3 / Tree-AR-trained model step, what does the headline cycle-saving translate to in step-time fraction? Even $f \in [10\%, 25\%]$ as a literature-cited range would suffice.
- **Q3 (new, deployment-classifier multiplier).** What fraction of *expected production* workloads sit at NL\%$\le$50\% vs NL\%$\ge$77\%? The deployment-classifier story would gain weight if the paper estimated, even qualitatively, what fraction of LLM workloads in 2024–2026 production fall in each regime — i.e., is this classifier saving architects "skip RL on rare workloads" or "skip RL on most workloads"? The answer changes the framing.
- **Q4 (new, W5 light touch).** Even without running NCCL hierarchical AllReduce, could you cite the 2D ring builder (NCCL topology-aware ring) and ZeRO++, and state whether express links substitute for or complement those collective-level optimizations? One paragraph in §II would defuse the most likely ISCA-systems reviewer follow-up.

## Decision

**ISCA: Borderline Accept (held from iter-14).** The half-step lift on Significance (2.0 → 2.5) is real but insufficient to push to a clean Weak Accept at top-tier. The single tipping-point experiment I have flagged for three iterations remains the same: a back-of-envelope Amdahl translation of the headline cycle-saving into Mixtral-8x7B or DeepSeek-V3 training-step time. That is one paragraph, one citation for the dispatch-collective fraction $f$, and a one-line arithmetic transformation. With that paragraph I would move to Weak Accept. Without it, the headline is still "ML-systems-flavored architecture work that the systems community will read appreciatively but cannot use to prioritize hardware investment against software alternatives," which is a defensible architecture-track contribution but not a top-tier LLM-systems-impact claim.

The same logic applies to the W3-framing gap: a single sentence in §V.C contrasting hardware express placement against software expert co-location would convert the MoE headline from "RL-WS solves Zipf skew" (which a Tutel reviewer will object to) into "RL-WS solves Zipf skew when the dispatcher cannot or chooses not to" (which is positioned and defensible). Cost: one sentence. Benefit: the framing gap closes. The fact that this has not been added across iter-14 and iter-15 is the one remaining authorial choice I would push back on.

**DAC/DATE: Accept (held from iter-14, strengthened conviction).** Iter-15 makes the architecture-track case stronger, not weaker. The wall-clock-cost table is exactly the kind of design-time deployability disclosure DAC/DATE rewards, and the PARL three-axis contrast positions the contribution cleanly against the closest prior work. The W4 Amdahl gap matters less here than at ISCA because end-to-end-LLM-training-step impact is not a hard prerequisite for an architecture-track contribution. This is a clean Accept at DAC/DATE.

**Honest calibration on the ISCA decision.** I considered moving to Weak Accept on the strength of the wall-clock disclosure plus the PARL contrast. The argument *for* moving is that iter-15 closes one full reviewer concern (design-time cost) and tightens another (related-work positioning) without introducing any technical regression, and that an iter-14 → iter-15 trajectory shows authors who respond to feedback iteration over iteration. The argument *against*, which I find more persuasive, is that the three-iteration-old W4 gap is the single concern that would change my own willingness to advocate for this paper in a programme committee discussion, and it is not closed. An ISCA reviewer with LLM-systems instinct will read §V.C, see the $-83.2\%$ MoE headline, and ask "out of what step?" — and the paper has no answer. Until that answer exists in any form (even a parametric range), Borderline Accept is the honest score.
